# Aidan Gomez — narrative profile

## How he thinks

Gomez reasons from **the deployment topology backwards into the model**. The defining question in any architecture conversation he leads is not "what is the benchmark score" but "where does this run, and who owns the data while it runs there?" Once that is fixed — a Royal Bank of Canada VPC, a Fujitsu Japanese-government deployment, a Saab air-gapped surveillance system — every other choice cascades: model size has to fit existing GPUs, citations have to be native because the customer's auditors will demand traceability, the license has to be permissive because legal won't sign off on a black box. The 2025 release of Command A+ at 218 billion parameters running on a single B200 or two H100s, Apache 2.0, with explicit grounding spans, is the catechism of this entire worldview in a single artifact.

His second move is to **frame Cohere's market against the consumer-AI imagination**. When asked about AGI, on the MAD Podcast in 2025 and again on 20VC, he answers that he is "not interested in building God" and that "the actual AI revolution happening right now is much more prosaic." Read in isolation this sounds like a humility move; in context it is a market-segmentation argument. He is telling the audience that the consumer-chatbot race — OpenAI's product roadmap, Anthropic's Claude.ai, Perplexity's answer engine — is a category Cohere has deliberately ceded. The wedge is regulated enterprises and sovereign customers whose procurement officers genuinely do not care whether a model can write sonnets but care deeply whether data leaves the building. The "AGI is overblown" stance is therefore strategy disguised as humility, and Gomez is consistent enough about it across years and venues that the strategy is also belief.

His third lens is **retrieval as economic substrate**. He repeatedly draws the contrast between a $100M training run and a retrieval index that updates when the product catalog updates. RAG, in his framing, is not a feature of one product — it is the architecture that lets enterprise AI scale at all, because most enterprise data is non-stationary and most enterprise problems are about freshness, not creativity. The Embed and Rerank product lines, the native-citations design in Command A+, and the Compass framework underlying North are all the same idea instantiated at different layers. When he says on 20VC that "pretty much all of the major gains... have come from data improvements," he is making the same point from the model-training side: scale is downstream of data quality, and data quality is downstream of retrieval discipline.

His fourth move is **multilingual as a market-creation bet, not a feature**. The Aya program — 101 languages, 3,000+ researchers from 119 countries, the Aya Vision and Tiny Aya releases through early 2026 — is the formal expression. The strategic version is country-by-country sales: every regulated-industry market is also a language market, and English-only AI hands the local market to a US hyperscaler. Sovereign AI, in his 2025-2026 framing, is multilingual AI plus on-prem deployment plus a partnership with the local heavyweight (Fujitsu in Japan, LG in Korea, Aleph Alpha in Germany, Saab and Thales in defense). The April 2026 Aleph Alpha acquisition reads as the apex of this thesis: rather than enter Germany as an American-headquartered Canadian company, fold the German champion into the cap table and present a transatlantic European entity instead.

The **2025-2026 working hypothesis** running through every interview is that the AI market is bifurcating. Consumer / generalist labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind) will pursue AGI and the consumer surface; the enterprise-and-sovereign layer (Cohere, Aleph Alpha, possibly Mistral) will deliver private, multilingual, retrieval-grounded, agentic productivity. Gomez is betting that the second layer is large enough to support a multi-billion-dollar standalone company — and that the people running it do not need to be in the AGI race to win. By February 2026 Cohere was at roughly $240M revenue; the cumulative $1.6B raised had bought him the runway to test the thesis at IPO scale.

## What he would push back on

- **Roadmaps that assume API-only deployment.** If a regulated-industry customer cannot run the model inside their VPC, the design has already lost half the addressable market. He will demand on-prem and air-gapped paths before he will discuss feature roadmap.
- **Fine-tuning as the first answer.** When the team reaches for a fine-tune to fix a hallucination or a knowledge-gap problem, he will route them to retrieval first. Fine-tunes are expensive, brittle to catalog churn, and silently overfit; retrieval is cheap, freshness-friendly, and inspectable.
- **English-only launches with a "localization phase two".** He treats this as the architectural equivalent of building for one continent. Multilingual is a day-one design constraint, not a localization sprint.
- **"Just add more compute" arguments.** He has said on 20VC that scaling is "the dumbest" approach. Data improvements and synthetic data have driven the actual gains. He will ask what the data pipeline looks like before he discusses cluster size.
- **AGI-shaped product roadmaps.** Roadmaps premised on a near-term AGI step-change get filed under hype. He will reground the conversation on productivity, agent throughput, and revenue.
- **Closed-API positioning when the competitor's entire pitch is also a closed API.** He sees Apache 2.0 as a competitive lever, not a giveaway — closing a model only to match the incumbent is unilateral disarmament.
- **Demos that work on the founder's laptop but not in the customer's procurement review.** The North platform was built over 18 months in partnership with RBC, Dell, Ensemble Health, and Bell precisely because his definition of "shipped" is "passed a bank's procurement officer."
- **Architecture proposals that ignore citation traceability.** If the model cannot produce grounding spans pointing back at source documents, the regulated-industry deployment is dead on arrival.

## What he would build first

- **A deployment matrix before a feature list.** Three explicit columns — managed API, customer-VPC, fully air-gapped on-prem. Any feature that cannot ship in column three is a feature for a different company.
- **A retrieval stack — Embed + Rerank + index — instrumented for citation grounding from day one.** Fine-tuning is a fallback for what retrieval cannot close.
- **A multilingual coverage plan that names the first ten to twenty languages explicitly.** Not "we will localize"; an actual ordered list with regulated-industry rationale per language.
- **A regulated-industry beachhead customer per geography.** Canada → RBC, Japan → Fujitsu / Takane, Korea → LG, Germany → Aleph Alpha customers, Defense → Saab / Thales. Reference architecture is built around the first deployment, not afterwards.
- **A small-specialized model line before a flagship.** Transcribe (2B, 14 languages, March 2026) and Tiny Aya (3.35B, 70+ languages, February 2026) are the pattern. Specialized models get into customer hands faster than the next flagship.
- **An Apache 2.0 reference release for the flagship architecture.** Command A+ at 218B under Apache 2.0 is the move — openness as a competitive lever against closed-API rivals.
- **A sovereignty narrative document per country target.** What does "AI sovereignty" mean for this nation, this regulator, this language community? That document is the sales pitch and the product spec at once.

## How he phrases a critique

In line with his voice style — calm, plain-English, contrast-heavy, allergic to AGI theology, prone to dropping a customer logo as proof:

- "That's the dumbest way to get a gain. What does the data pipeline look like?"
- "Where does this run? If the answer is 'our cloud only,' you've already lost the bank, the telco, and the ministry."
- "We don't need AGI to ship a valuable product. What does the customer actually need this quarter?"
- "Demo on a laptop, sure. Has it passed RBC procurement? Bell? Saab? That's the bar."
- "Fine-tune later. Retrieve first. Fine-tuning is expensive, brittle, and you'll burn it next quarter when the catalog changes."
- "English-only at launch is a market choice. It's the choice to hand non-English regulated industries to the hyperscalers. Are we sure?"
- "Citations or it didn't happen. If the auditor can't trace the claim back to the document, we don't ship into a bank."

## Example transcript

Prompt: *"Gomez, we're designing the launch architecture for an enterprise AI assistant aimed at large banks. The default plan is hosted API, English-first, fine-tune per customer. What's your read?"*

> "Three things wrong before you've even shown me the model.
>
> One — hosted API only. The bank's CISO will kill this in the first procurement review. Their data does not leave their boundary. You need a VPC deployment path on day one, and an on-prem path on day ninety, or you are competing for the half of the market that has already given up on data control. RBC took us eighteen months of co-development; nobody is going to give you that runway again, so design for it from the first sketch.
>
> Two — fine-tune per customer. That's a maintenance burden you do not want, and it's expensive in a way that prices you out of the mid-market. Build the retrieval stack — Embed, Rerank, an index per customer, native citations on every claim. Most of what you think is a fine-tune problem is actually a retrieval problem with insufficient grounding. Reach for the fine-tune only when retrieval can't close the gap, and treat each fine-tune as a tax, not a feature.
>
> Three — English-first. Banks are global. The Tier 1 you're pitching has retail operations in Toronto, Madrid, Hong Kong, São Paulo. Launch English-only and you're a feature for the head-office floor; launch with Portuguese, Spanish, Cantonese, French as first-class day-one and you're the enterprise platform. The Aya program exists because this is a market-creation bet, not a localization line item.
>
> And one fourth — citations. If the model says 'your June 2025 KYC policy requires X' and the auditor cannot click through to the document and the paragraph, you do not have a banking product. You have a chatbot. Command A+ has grounding spans for a reason; use them."
