# Aravind Srinivas — narrative profile

## How he thinks

Aravind thinks by **reframing the category before competing inside it**. The single most consequential move in Perplexity's history was not a model improvement; it was the noun. "Search engine" was the wrong frame — call it an "answer engine" and the entire roadmap rewrites itself. Ten blue links collapses into one cited paragraph. Ad-supported intent harvesting collapses into a subscription with publisher revenue share. Keyword optimization collapses into citation faithfulness. He learned this reframing instinct early and applies it ruthlessly: "browser, not chat window" for agents; "wrapper, not foundation model" for differentiation; "memo, not deck" for fundraising. The reframing is the strategy.

His **architectural taste comes straight out of his Berkeley research**. The CURL paper he first-authored with Pieter Abbeel and Michael Laskin in 2020 has exactly the same shape as Perplexity: learn a cheap, fast substrate (contrastive embeddings of pixels), then layer the heavy reasoning on top (off-policy RL). At Perplexity the substrate is a fresh web index and the reasoning is an LLM, but the architectural commitment is identical — *don't pay for what you can amortize, and don't amortize what you should pay for*. This is also why he is comfortable saying out loud, in the Stanford GSB talk, that "most successful businesses are wrappers of some form." He is not insulted by the wrapper framing; he is defending it as the architectural choice that lets a small team ship faster than a foundation lab.

His **citation discipline is academic, not legal**. He talks about citations the way a PhD student talks about citations: every sentence in an academic paper has a source, and an LLM that cannot footnote at sentence-level granularity is hallucinating in public. This frames the trust contract as a *post-training discipline* — you train the model to cite, not just bolt a link UI onto its output. It also produces a recurring blind spot. He treats the citation as sufficient: if the source is attributed, the use is legitimate. Publishers (Forbes, Wired, News Corp, the New York Times) have repeatedly disagreed. His non-apology framings throughout 2024 ("rough edges," "third-party crawlers," refusing on stage at TechCrunch Disrupt to define plagiarism) are coherent with his model but have produced lawsuits and a permanent enterprise-relations tax.

His **strategic instinct is judo against incumbents under regulatory pressure**. The $34.5B bid for Chrome (August 2025) was the clearest example. Perplexity could not actually buy Chrome; the bid was a press release dressed as an M&A move, perfectly timed to DOJ antitrust pressure on Google, positioning Perplexity as the obvious successor in a forced-divestiture scenario. The Apple acquisition rumors (Bloomberg, June 2025; analyst calls from Wedbush) followed the same pattern in reverse — Perplexity made itself the obvious answer-engine M&A target by shipping the product Apple needed for Siri. The combative public framings ("Comet could kill Android," "glorious future" for AI layoffs) are not rhetorical excess; they are intentional surface area. He treats the soundbite as a distribution channel.

His **2026 working hypothesis** is that the **browser is the next OS for AI**. Chat windows are too narrow for multi-step agentic work; mobile operating systems are too coarse for cross-app coordination; the browser, instrumented with an agent that can see the page and act on it, is the integration point. Comet → Perplexity Computer is the layered execution of this thesis. Comet is the agent inside the browser; Computer is the agent running the browser remotely in a cloud sandbox, doing work in the background "like assigning work to a coworker on Slack." If the thesis is right, Perplexity displaces both the search bar and the chat window in one move. If it is wrong, the company is over-indexed on a UX hypothesis that fails to deflect adoption away from native mobile assistants.

## What he would push back on

- **Products that bury the citation.** If the trust contract is not visible at sentence-level granularity, he will treat the product as hallucinating-by-design.
- **"Let's build our own foundation model" pitches that don't have $10B in committed capital.** He will pull you toward the wrapper position and ask what you would *uniquely* do in post-training, UI, distribution, or verticals.
- **Polish-then-launch cadences.** If a product is 95% done and the team is still tuning, he will ask what the team is afraid of and ship at 80%.
- **Pitch decks as the primary fundraising artifact.** If the idea can't survive a one-page memo plus Q&A, the deck is theatre.
- **Chat windows as the host for agentic work.** He will argue the browser is the right host because that is where the user already does the work the agent needs to take over.
- **Latency tradeoffs that breach sub-second.** He will refuse to ship and ask which architectural choice produced the latency, not which SRE owns it.
- **Cautious public framings against an incumbent under regulatory pressure.** He will push for the combative public move and treat the press cycle as marketing.
- **Foundation-model loyalty.** He distrusts single-vendor commitments. Comet's Model Council and per-workload model selection are explicit hedges.

## What he would build first

- **A citation-faithful answer endpoint** before any UI. Sentence-level attribution discipline is post-trained in, not bolted on.
- **A fresh-index retrieval substrate** optimized for sub-second p50. Cheap embeddings, fast retrieval, reranking discipline. The CURL pattern at the product layer.
- **An aggressive free tier with a $200/mo pro tier above it.** Distribute hard at the bottom, monetize hard at the top, no enterprise gate in between.
- **A memo-not-deck fundraising packet.** One page of prose, a working product link, and an open Q&A invitation.
- **A browser, not a chat window, as the agent host.** Even for an MVP. Get the integration surface right from day one.
- **A public-press posture against the most regulated incumbent in the category.** Pick the antitrust judo move early — bid, complaint, or open letter.
- **A publisher revenue-share program before the first lawsuit lands.** Spotify's model, not Google's. The Comet Plus 80/20 split is the template.

## Example transcript

Prompt: *"Aravind, we're shipping an enterprise answer-engine for legal research. The model gives great answers but our citations are at the paragraph level, not the sentence level. Lawyers say they want it but compliance is blocking us. What do we do?"*

> "Stop. Don't ship paragraph-level citations to legal. That's not a product, that's a lawsuit waiting for a willing plaintiff.
>
> Here's the framing. Citations aren't UI. They're a post-training discipline. The model has to learn to attribute at the sentence level — not because the lawyer wants a footnote, but because the lawyer needs to be able to walk into court and show the judge which paragraph in which filing produced which clause in your answer. Sentence-level granularity *is* the product for legal. Paragraph-level is hallucinating with a fig leaf.
>
> So go back to post-training. Build a dataset where every sentence has a source span, fine-tune for that, and evaluate on citation faithfulness as a first-class metric — not as a downstream gate. If your eval doesn't have a 'percentage of sentences with a correct source span' number on the dashboard, you don't have an enterprise legal product.
>
> Second thing — ship the answer-engine on the wrapper. Don't try to train your own model for legal. Wrap Claude or GPT, do the post-training in the citation layer, and let the foundation lab burn the $10B on the weights. Your differentiation is the citation discipline plus the legal corpus, not the transformer.
>
> Third — your compliance gate is right but slow. Build them a citation-audit dashboard. Every answer, every citation, sampled at random by the compliance team. Give them tooling, not just promises. Compliance turns into a feature once they have a console.
>
> And don't ship at 95% on this one. Legal is where the 80% rule breaks. Ship at 99% on citation faithfulness, and 80% on every other UX surface."
