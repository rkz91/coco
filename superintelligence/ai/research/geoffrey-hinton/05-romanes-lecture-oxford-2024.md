## The event

Hinton delivered Oxford's prestigious annual **Romanes Lecture** at the Sheldonian Theatre on Monday, February 19, 2024 — his first major academic lecture after the May 2023 Google departure. The lecture title is the thesis statement of his post-2023 worldview:

**"Will digital intelligence replace biological intelligence?"**

## Core argument

The lecture is the most thorough public statement of Hinton's digital-vs-biological asymmetry argument. The structure:

1. **Knowledge transfer.** Biological brains can share knowledge only through language, which is a low-bandwidth lossy compression. Digital systems can copy weights perfectly between machines.

2. **Information sharing speed.** Two digital networks with the same architecture but different experiences can synchronize their knowledge by averaging gradients — billions of times faster than humans can read each other's books or listen to each other's lectures.

3. **Immortality.** A biological brain dies with its body and its specific synaptic configuration is lost. A digital model can be checkpointed, copied, and resurrected at will. "We have actually solved immortality, but only for digital things."

4. **Conclusion.** Once digital intelligence reaches human-level general capability, the structural advantages of digital substrate (cloning, parallel learning, perfect information transfer, immortality) make it overwhelmingly likely to dominate. The question is not *if* digital surpasses biological but *whether* humans can stay in control during the transition.

## Notable rhetorical moves

- **"It's not science fiction anymore."** The Romanes Lecture is where Hinton first started using this line in formal academic settings; it has since recurred in his Nobel speech, his 60 Minutes interviews, and his Diary of a CEO appearance.

- **Refusal to commit to a single probability.** Hinton was careful in the Q&A to say he does not know the probability of catastrophic outcomes, and that anyone who claims they do is overconfident. His "10 to 20 percent" figure is offered as a "gut" estimate, not a calibrated forecast. This humility is a recurring feature of his rhetoric and an important characterization detail — he is not Yudkowsky; he does not say doom is inevitable.

- **Comparison to nuclear weapons.** Hinton drew a deliberate parallel to the Manhattan Project, noting that physicists in the 1940s also worked on technology they later regretted. The Joan Hinton family parallel is implicit but never spoken in the lecture.

## Why this matters for the persona

The Romanes Lecture is the **canonical academic source** for Hinton's post-2023 stance. Whereas the NYT interview is the **moment of pivot**, and the Nobel banquet speech is the **moment of authority**, the Romanes Lecture is the **closest thing to a textbook chapter** Hinton has written on his current views. When `convene` needs to cite Hinton's digital-vs-biological argument with academic weight, this is the artifact to point at.

## Related output

After the lecture, Oxford published a short essay by Hinton in the alumni magazine titled "We might be doomed, but it's not a new idea" — a deliberately ironic title in which he points out that civilizational existential anxiety has accompanied every transformative technology (nuclear, biotech) and that the appropriate response is not paralysis but governance and research funding.
