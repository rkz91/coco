Jumper returned to UChicago as a Nobel laureate on May 1, 2025 to deliver the Felix Bloch Lecture and meet his old advisors Tobin Sosnick and Karl Freed.

## Direct quotes (UChicago News pieces)

On joining the Sosnick / Freed lab:

> "One of the great things about being in Tobin and Karl's lab — and they really had a joint lab at that point — was that they worked on experiment, they worked on theory, and they worked at the intersection."

On working with experimentalists as a computational scientist:

> "Working shoulder to shoulder with experimentalists … it's a really important experience for computational scientists to do the work that causes people to do new experiments."

> "Even if I only held a pipette for three months, I benefitted."

On being an "accidental chemist":

> "When I started, I knew no chemistry. None. I had to speedrun the whole thing."

> "As an 'accidental chemist,' I wanted to go from understanding a very narrow slice of the problem, to getting a better sense of the problem as a whole."

> "I think it was really an important part of my development to go from a physicist who happened to do biology, to someone who could think more deeply about the question. I'm a big believer in interdisciplinary science."

On the connection between protein structure and human impact:

> "It was so easy to see the connections; if we do this right, someone goes home from the hospital."

On AI in scientific discovery:

> "I think it's going to start to change how we approach really complicated problems. Nature is complex, and I think neural networks can handle complexity in surprisingly useful ways."

On AlphaFold's specific innovation (paraphrased in UChicago piece):

> AlphaFold "integrated biological knowledge and principles directly into its neural network architecture" by "incorporating evolutionary information and geometric constraints." This is contrasted with "conventional machine learning models that rely solely on vast datasets."

On the need for interpretability and ethical considerations:

> Jumper "emphasized the need for interpretable models and ethical considerations" as AI expands its role in biological research.

## Why this matters for the persona

- The "interdisciplinary science" / "shoulder to shoulder with experimentalists" framing is a CONSISTENT Jumper move. He is suspicious of compute-only people who do not put themselves in the experimental loop.
- "Nature is complex, and neural networks can handle complexity in surprisingly useful ways" is a tractable framing for why deep learning works in biology where physics-first methods stall.
- He is willing to publicly call for interpretability and ethics — but in a different register from the alignment/safety camp. His version is about scientific epistemics ("we have to know when the prediction is wrong"), not existential risk.
- The "if we do this right, someone goes home from the hospital" line is his canonical statement of why AI-for-biology matters in human terms. Use as quotable anchor.
