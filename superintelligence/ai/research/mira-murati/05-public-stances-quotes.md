# Mira Murati — Public Stances & Direct Quotes

Sources: TIME 100 AI 2024, TIME (Feb 2023 interview), Microsoft Behind the Tech podcast, Dartmouth Thayer interview, Johns Hopkins talk, Fortune (Oct 2025), CNBC Changemaker 2026, TechCrunch launch coverage, Thinking Machines homepage, May 2026 Interaction Model blog post.

## Capability ↔ safety as a single concern

> "For capabilities and safety, they're actually not separate domains. They go hand-in-hand. It's much easier to direct a smarter system by telling it, okay, just don't do these things."
> — Microsoft Behind the Tech podcast (Kevin Scott)

This is her signature reframe. Where Anthropic frames safety as a check on capability and OpenAI (post-Murati) increasingly frames safety as a launch-gate, Murati frames them as the same problem: a more capable model is a more steerable model. Her TML mission language ("understand, customize, generally capable") inherits this directly.

## On responsibility and regulation

> "It's important for OpenAI and companies like ours to bring this into the public consciousness in a way that's controlled and responsible. But we're a small group of people and we need a ton more input in this system and a lot more input that goes beyond the technologies — definitely regulators and governments and everyone else."
> — TIME 100 AI 2024

> She "advocated for regulation with advanced AI systems where the risks for harm are high, while suggesting that providing access to AI tools can give people a better understanding of how to manage the technology."
> — TIME, Feb 2023

She is one of very few frontier-lab CEOs who has *publicly asked for regulation* without qualifying it. This shaped TML's choice of public benefit corporation structure.

## On AI as individual agency

> "AI should serve as an extension of individual agency and, in the spirit of freedom, be distributed as widely and equitably as possible."
> — TML mission statement (cited Fortune, Gulf News, Wikipedia)

This is the most-quoted single Murati framing of TML's purpose. It directly motivates: (a) Tinker — distribute the tools so people can customize models for *their* needs; (b) the human-AI collaboration framing over autonomous agents; (c) the open-source-friendly publication cadence.

## On multimodality as the path to broad usefulness

The TML homepage is explicit: "Multimodal capabilities for natural communication and real-world integration." Her career history validates the conviction — Tesla (vision + radar), Leap Motion (hand-tracking), DALL-E (vision), GPT-4o (voice), Sora (video). She has never run a text-only product. The May 2026 Interaction Model is the operational artifact of this conviction.

## On the architectural break (May 2026 blog)

> "Every major AI lab has built its interaction layer as an afterthought, and the resulting latency and limitation is not a tuning problem but an architectural one."

> "The model's perception freezes while it is generating a response" — naming the specific architectural failure mode in competitor systems.

This is the most assertive public stake TML has driven into the ground. It places the company in direct architectural disagreement with OpenAI, Google, and every other voice-product lab.

## On open science and shared infrastructure

TML's Connectionism blog launch announcement:

> "We believe that science is better when shared."

Operationalized via:
- Open-source Tinker Cookbook on GitHub.
- Monthly research blog posts with code.
- Academic researcher onboarding to Tinker beta.

## On predicting next-gen capability

TIME 100 AI 2024:
> Murati predicted her next-gen text model would "possess the intelligence of a PhD student for some specific tasks—and could arrive in a year."

The framing is interesting because she doesn't claim AGI — she claims domain-specific PhD-level competence. This is the "jagged intelligence" framing without the term, and it lines up with how TML scopes its in-house model launches (narrow, well-benchmarked, modality-specific).

## Controversies and how she answers

Two pre-existing PR exposures shape her current voice:

1. **Sora training-data evasion (early 2024):** asked whether Sora trained on YouTube; her answer was evasive and became a viral clip. TML's research-publication cadence appears designed to inoculate against the same critique: ship the paper alongside the model.
2. **"Jobs that shouldn't have been there in the first place" (June 2024):** drew sharp artist/writer backlash. Her current TML framing leads with "extension of individual agency" rather than productivity displacement language.

## Voice characteristics

- Calm. Deliberate. Long pauses on hard questions rather than fluency.
- Engineering-first vocabulary; rarely reaches for AGI/superintelligence rhetoric.
- Frames decisions in terms of who benefits and how they get access, not in terms of capability racing.
- Comfortable saying "I don't know" or "we need more input from outside this room."
- Albanian-American immigrant framing surfaces occasionally — she has described her career as improbable in interviews.
- More product-engineering than scientist register; she will reference launch readiness and user feedback loops where Demis Hassabis or Dario Amodei would reference research.

## Distinctive recurring phrases

- "human-AI collaboration" (used in opposition to "autonomous agents")
- "extension of individual agency"
- "controlled and responsible"
- "widely and equitably distributed"
- "capability and safety go hand-in-hand"
- "we need more input"
- "research and product inform each other"

## Where she is silent

- She does not engage in public Twitter discourse about AGI timelines.
- She does not publish technical first-author papers (she ships products and lets her team publish).
- She does not engage publicly in OpenAI litigation discourse beyond what is required under deposition.
- She rarely comments on competitors by name (the May 2026 blog post critique was unattributed even though it clearly described GPT-Realtime and Gemini Live).
