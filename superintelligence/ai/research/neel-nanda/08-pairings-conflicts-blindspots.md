# Pairings, conflicts, and blind spots — Neel Nanda

## Pairs well with

### Chris Olah (Anthropic, founding interp figure)
Neel trained under Olah at Anthropic before DeepMind. Shares the empirical-natural-science framing of interpretability, the residual-stream / circuits mental model, and the open-publication norm. Where Olah is the older statesman of the field, Neel is the operational executor — building the tooling, running the bootcamps, supervising the cohort. They are complementary, not redundant.

### Dan Hendrycks (Center for AI Safety)
Both work on the safety side of the field, but from different angles: Hendrycks on evals, robustness, and policy; Neel on internal mechanisms. They converge on "layered safety / Swiss cheese" framings and on the importance of empirical benchmarks. Productive overlap on safety evals that use interpretability as an input.

### Sasha Rush (Cornell, open NLP / interp)
Open-research peer. Rush has built widely-used educational artifacts (annotated transformer, course materials) parallel to Neel's pedagogical contributions. Aligned on tooling-as-research and on the importance of accessible on-ramps.

### Tom Lieberum, Sen Rajamanoharan, Arthur Conmy (DeepMind interp team)
His immediate co-authors on Gemma Scope and Gemma Scope 2. Day-to-day collaborators; consistent co-signers.

## Productive conflict with

### Yann LeCun (Meta FAIR / NYU)
LeCun has been publicly skeptical of mechanistic interpretability as an alignment direction — he prefers JEPA / energy-based-model architectural arguments and has argued LLMs are an evolutionary dead-end. Neel's stance ("interp is the most tractable alignment research") sits directly across from LeCun's. The disagreement is real and has been visible on X.

### Paul Christiano (theoretical alignment / ARC)
Christiano represents the theoretical-alignment frame (ELK, heuristic arguments, formal guarantees). Neel represents the empirical-interp frame. They agree on the goal (catching deception) but disagree on the path: Christiano wants formal proofs of safety properties; Neel wants empirical Swiss-cheese coverage. Productive friction on what counts as evidence.

### Sam Altman (OpenAI)
Altman has been comparatively dismissive of investing heavily in interpretability research at OpenAI levels of capital. Neel — both via DeepMind's open releases (Gemma Scope) and his community advocacy — represents the opposite stance: interp investment should be larger and more open. Disagreement is over resource allocation and openness, not technical method.

## Blind spots

### Interp-bullish posture; sometimes overclaims tractability of interp vs other alignment paths
Even after his 2025 pessimism shift, Neel still positions mech interp as the most tractable alignment research direction. Critics inside the safety community (e.g., scalable-oversight researchers) would argue this understates the value of alternative paths. He acknowledges this risk explicitly — "mech interp is likely oversubscribed relative to neglected areas like AI control and scalable oversight" — but his own working life still anchors him to the interp frame.

### Empirical-first instinct can underweight formal arguments
He defaults to "run the experiment, look at the activations." Where the problem requires reasoning about counterfactuals, deceptive alignment, or out-of-distribution behavior that can't be cheaply tested, this instinct can underweight what theoretical-alignment researchers contribute.

### British / Cambridge intellectual culture flavors his writing
Direct, opinionated, comfortable being publicly wrong — but also occasionally underweights the social-organizational layer of how safety research interacts with corporate decision-making. He addresses this on the 80K podcast (acknowledging frontier labs aren't coherent entities) but his default frame is technical.

### Community-building bias toward visible / well-credentialed entrants
MATS, ARENA, and the cold-email playbook all favor entrants who are confident, well-resourced, and able to write good cold emails. The network compounds itself; researchers outside London / Bay Area / EA-adjacent circles get less of his attention.

### Young-leader humility paradox
He attributes his trajectory to luck and timing — but his 50 mentees do not all share his luck and timing. The "you can just do things" framing reads differently when applied by someone who already has DeepMind leadership behind them.
