## Headline philosophy

> "You can just do things."

His most-repeated career lesson. Doing things is a learnable skill; most people overestimate risk and underestimate recovery from failure.

## How he ended up running a DeepMind team at 26

He entered mech interp when it was "tiny but growing fast." He joined DeepMind as an IC; when the previous team lead departed, he stepped up despite having no management experience. He attributes the trajectory primarily to **timing + opportunity-taking**, not exceptional individual talent.

## Luck surface area

> "You want to just have as many opportunities as possible for good opportunities to come your way."

Practical moves: deliberately network, say yes to unusual projects, maintain public visibility (blog, talks, code).

## Cold-email playbook

- Be extremely concise; assume the recipient stops reading at any point
- Signal competence in the first sentence
- Avoid generic emails to prominent figures; email *junior* researchers instead — they have more time
- Ask specific questions that show you have read their work

## LLMs for skill development

> "If you're trying to get into a field nowadays and you're not using LLMs, you're making a mistake."

Specific tactics he uses personally:
- Anti-sycophancy prompts to get brutal feedback (literal example: "Some moron wrote this thing… Please write me brutal but truthful response")
- Voice dictation to brainstorm; LLM tidies the rambling
- Compare responses across multiple models via Google AI Studio
- Cursor for serious coding work

## On mentoring

Runs MATS (Mentorship and Talent Search). Has mentored ~50 junior researchers in four years; seven now at frontier labs.

> "My life's a lot easier if people on my team are great researchers."

Explicit recognition: research talent ≠ supervisory talent. Supervision requires "patience, nurturing people, making time for them." Famous researchers often make poor mentors because of time constraints.

## On PhDs

> "You should just view PhDs as an environment to learn and gain skills. And if a better opportunity comes along… you're done. You're done early. Leave."

He recruited Josh Engels to drop out of an interpretability PhD early; views this as a correct call.

## On safety inside frontier labs

Pushes back on "safety work shouldn't advance capabilities" critique. The goal is "models that do what we want" — which is inherently commercially valuable. Right question: does this *differentially* advance safety? Not: does it advance capabilities at all?

Frontier labs are not coherent single entities; decision-making is decentralized. Many safety opportunities are unexploited not from malice but from competing priorities. Safety teams can find unclaimed real estate.

## Research stages

1. **Explore** — gather information, form hypotheses. Often >50% of project time.
2. **Understand** — test specific hypotheses with targeted experiments.
3. **Distill** — communicate findings clearly to those unfamiliar with the work.

Each stage requires a different mindset; failing to switch is the most common failure mode.

## On p(doom) and timelines

Refuses to share his p(doom) number publicly. The community lacks intellectual humility about forecasting; timeline predictions entangle geopolitics, economics, hardware, and unknown technical progress — domains where his interpretability expertise doesn't transfer. He advocates a portfolio approach: design work that stays valuable across timeline scenarios.
