# 02 — Stanford Commencement Address, June 12, 2005

Source: Steve Jobs Archive (https://stevejobsarchive.com/stories/stay-hungry-stay-foolish), Rev transcript (https://www.rev.com/transcripts/steve-jobs-stanford-commencement-speech-transcript-2005), Farnam Street commentary (https://fs.blog/steve-jobs-stanford-commencement/), Fox News transcript republication, and consensus excerpts confirmed across multiple secondary sources.

Approximate length: 15 minutes. The most-watched commencement address in history — over 120 million views across distribution channels per the Steve Jobs Archive's twentieth-anniversary materials (June 2025). Delivered at Stanford Stadium to the class of 2005.

## Structural Skeleton

Jobs opens by acknowledging he never graduated college and that the Stanford commencement is the closest he has come to a college graduation. He then says: "Today I want to tell you three stories from my life. That's it. No big deal. Just three stories."

### Story 1 — Connecting the Dots

**Setup:** Biological mother put him up for adoption on the condition the adoptive parents would be college graduates. They weren't (his adoptive father had not finished high school), but they signed a pledge that Steve would attend college. He enrolled at Reed College ("almost as expensive as Stanford"), realized after six months he had "no idea what I wanted to do with my life and no idea how college was going to help me figure it out," and dropped out.

**The calligraphy detour:** After dropping out, he stayed at Reed as an unofficial auditor for 18 months. He audited Robert Palladino's calligraphy class. "I learned about serif and sans-serif typefaces, about varying the amount of space between different letter combinations, about what makes great typography great."

**The payoff line:** "If I had never dropped in on that single calligraphy course in college, the Mac would have never had multiple typefaces or proportionally spaced fonts. And since Windows just copied the Mac, it's likely that no personal computer would have them."

**The crystallized teaching:** "You can't connect the dots looking forward; you can only connect them looking backwards. So you have to trust that the dots will somehow connect in your future. You have to trust in something — your gut, destiny, life, karma, whatever. This approach has never let me down, and it has made all the difference in my life."

### Story 2 — Love and Loss

**Setup:** "I was lucky. I found what I loved to do early in life. Woz and I started Apple in my parents' garage when I was 20. We worked hard, and in 10 years Apple had grown from just the two of us in a garage into a $2 billion company with over 4,000 employees. We had just released our finest creation, the Macintosh, a year earlier, and I had just turned 30. And then I got fired."

**The firing (1985):** "How can you get fired from a company you started? Well, as Apple grew we hired someone who I thought was very talented to run the company with me, and for the first year or so things went well. But then our visions of the future began to diverge and eventually we had a falling out. When we did, our Board of Directors sided with him. So at 30 I was out. And very publicly out."

**The recovery:** "I didn't see it then, but it turned out that getting fired from Apple was the best thing that could have ever happened to me. The heaviness of being successful was replaced by the lightness of being a beginner again, less sure about everything. It freed me to enter one of the most creative periods of my life."

**What came next:** Founded NeXT (1985), acquired Pixar (1986), met Laurene Powell. NeXT's technology became the foundation of macOS when Apple bought NeXT in 1996. Pixar made *Toy Story* in 1995, "now the most successful animation studio in the world."

**The crystallized teaching:** "I'm convinced that the only thing that kept me going was that I loved what I did. You've got to find what you love. And that is as true for your work as it is for your lovers. Your work is going to fill a large part of your life, and the only way to be truly satisfied is to do what you believe is great work. And the only way to do great work is to love what you do. If you haven't found it yet, keep looking. Don't settle. As with all matters of the heart, you'll know when you find it."

### Story 3 — Death

**The setup:** "When I was 17, I read a quote that went something like: 'If you live each day as if it was your last, someday you'll most certainly be right.' It made an impression on me, and since then, for the past 33 years, I have looked in the mirror every morning and asked myself: 'If today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I am about to do today?' And whenever the answer has been 'No' for too many days in a row, I know I need to change something."

**The diagnosis:** "About a year ago I was diagnosed with cancer. I had a scan at 7:30 in the morning, and it clearly showed a tumor on my pancreas. I didn't even know what a pancreas was. The doctors told me this was almost certainly a type of cancer that is incurable, and that I should expect to live no longer than three to six months."

**The reprieve:** A biopsy later that day revealed the tumor was a rare form of pancreatic neuroendocrine cancer that was surgically curable. Jobs underwent the Whipple procedure in July 2004 and at the time of the speech (June 2005) was "fine."

**The crystallized teaching:** "No one wants to die. Even people who want to go to heaven don't want to die to get there. And yet death is the destination we all share. No one has ever escaped it. And that is as it should be, because Death is very likely the single best invention of Life. It is Life's change agent. It clears out the old to make way for the new. Right now the new is you, but someday not too long from now, you will gradually become the old and be cleared away. Sorry to be so dramatic, but it is quite true."

**The directive:** "Your time is limited, so don't waste it living someone else's life. Don't be trapped by dogma — which is living with the results of other people's thinking. Don't let the noise of others' opinions drown out your own inner voice. And most important, have the courage to follow your heart and intuition. They somehow already know what you truly want to become. Everything else is secondary."

### The Close

He references *The Whole Earth Catalog* by Stewart Brand: "It was sort of like Google in paperback form, 35 years before Google came along." On the back cover of the final issue (1974), beneath a photograph of an early-morning country road, were the words: "Stay Hungry. Stay Foolish."

**Closing line:** "And I have always wished that for myself. And now, as you graduate to begin anew, I wish that for you. Stay Hungry. Stay Foolish. Thank you all very much."

## Why This Speech Anchors the Persona

The Stanford speech is the single most accessible, widely-watched, plainspoken Jobs artifact. For a persona profile, four signature moves and three mental models trace directly to it:

1. **"Connecting the dots backwards"** — a complete mental model in three words. The justification for following curiosity rather than market research.
2. **"Stay hungry. Stay foolish."** — the closing voice signature. The phrase pre-dates Jobs (Whole Earth Catalog, 1974) but he owns it now.
3. **"If today were the last day of my life..."** — the daily-discipline framing of the focus-and-saying-no instinct.
4. **"You've got to find what you love."** — the love-of-craft framing that explains his refusal to delegate product decisions.
5. **"Don't be trapped by dogma — which is living with the results of other people's thinking."** — the contrarian instinct articulated plainly.
