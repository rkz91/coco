# 04 — iPhone Keynote, January 9, 2007 (Macworld)

Source: Apple Newsroom announcement ("Apple Reinvents the Phone with iPhone," January 9, 2007), Apple Press Releases archive, European Rhetoric annotated transcript (european-rhetoric.com/analyses/ikeynote-analysis-iphone/transcript-2007/), Time Magazine retrospective (time.com/4628515/steve-jobs-iphone-launch-keynote-2007/), the All About Steve Jobs keynote archive (allaboutstevejobs.com/videos/keynotes/macworld_2007).

The keynote is approximately 80 minutes long. It is widely considered the most consequential single product launch in technology history.

## Opening Frame

Jobs walks on stage at the Moscone Center in San Francisco. His opening line:

> "This is a day I've been looking forward to for two and a half years."

Then the framing claim:

> "Every once in a while a revolutionary product comes along that changes everything. And Apple has been — well, first of all, one's very fortunate if you get to work on just one of these in your career. Apple's been very fortunate. It's been able to introduce a few of these into the world: in 1984, we introduced the Macintosh. It didn't just change Apple, it changed the whole computer industry. In 2001, we introduced the first iPod. And it didn't just change the way we all listened to music, it changed the entire music industry. Well, today, we're introducing three revolutionary products of this class."

## The Triple Misdirection

This is the moment Jobs becomes a textbook case study in dramatic structure. He sets up three separate products, each presented in turn:

> "The first one is a widescreen iPod with touch controls. The second is a revolutionary mobile phone. And the third is a breakthrough internet communications device."

He repeats each three times, building the audience's expectation of three separate products. Then:

> "So, three things: a widescreen iPod with touch controls, a revolutionary mobile phone, and a breakthrough internet communications device. An iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator. An iPod, a phone — are you getting it? These are not three separate devices. This is one device. And we are calling it iPhone."

The audience reaction (roar of applause) is preserved on the canonical video. It is the moment the smartphone era begins.

## The Reframing of the Mobile Phone Market

After the reveal Jobs walks through the existing smartphone market (Blackberry, Treo, Nokia E62, Motorola Q) and the criticism he is about to apply:

> "The problem is they're not so smart and they're not so easy to use. What we want to do is make a leapfrog product that is way smarter than any mobile device has ever been, and super-easy to use. This is what iPhone is."

He then walks through the multi-touch user interface:

> "We are all born with the ultimate pointing device — our fingers — and iPhone uses them to create the most revolutionary user interface since the mouse."

And he explicitly attacks the physical-keyboard, hardware-button approach of competitors:

> "We don't want to carry around a mouse, right? So what are we going to do? Oh, a stylus, right? We're gonna use a stylus. No. Who wants a stylus? You have to get them and put them away, and you lose them. Yuck. Nobody wants a stylus. So let's not use a stylus."

## The Apple Press Release Pull-Quote (January 9, 2007)

The official Apple Newsroom announcement, which Jobs personally edited per Isaacson:

> "iPhone is a revolutionary and magical product that is literally five years ahead of any other mobile phone. We are all born with the ultimate pointing device — our fingers — and iPhone uses them to create the most revolutionary user interface since the mouse." — Steve Jobs, Apple's CEO

## The Closing Reframe

Jobs closes the keynote with one more reframing slide: the Apple Computer Inc. corporate name change. Mid-keynote, he announces that effective immediately the company will drop "Computer" from its name and become simply Apple Inc. The signal is that iPhone is not a side project — it is the new center of the company.

## What This Keynote Reveals About the Jobs Decision Frame

Five structural moves visible in the keynote, each transferable to an AI product launch context:

1. **The "every once in a while" frame**: position the product in historical-stakes terms. Mac → iPod → iPhone. Each is a category creator, not a feature update.
2. **The triple misdirection**: hide the synthesis until the audience has independently understood each piece. The "are you getting it?" beat is the moment the audience does the work of integration in their own head.
3. **The competitor demolition by attacking their interaction model, not their features**: Jobs does not say the Blackberry has fewer features; he says it requires you to use a stylus or hard keyboard, and that the right pointer is your finger. Reframing the basis of comparison is the move.
4. **The stylus rejection**: explicit elimination of the obvious solution. The opposite of feature-stuffing.
5. **The corporate identity change as part of the launch**: "Apple Computer Inc. → Apple Inc." is a one-slide signal that this is not a side bet. The product launch *is* the strategic announcement.

For an AI persona that maps Jobs' product instincts onto modern AI launches, the parallel is striking. A new AI capability launch in the Jobs mold would: position the work at historical stakes; integrate three previously-separate capabilities into a single artifact; explicitly attack the dominant interaction model (chat? prompt? API?) rather than out-feature it; and re-name or re-position the company so that the launch is structurally weight-bearing.
