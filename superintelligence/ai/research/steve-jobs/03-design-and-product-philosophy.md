# 03 — Design and Product Philosophy

Synthesized from the 2003 New York Times Magazine interview by Rob Walker ("The Guts of a New Machine"), the iPad 2010 keynote, the iPhone 2007 keynote, the 1995 Robert Cringely "Lost Interview," the 1996 Wired interview with Gary Wolf, Walter Isaacson's biography, and Steve Jobs Archive materials.

## "Design is how it works"

The signature design-philosophy quote, from a November 30, 2003 New York Times Magazine profile of the iPod by Rob Walker:

> "Most people make the mistake of thinking design is what it looks like. People think it's this veneer — that the designers are handed this box and told, 'Make it look good!' That's not what we think design is. It's not just what it looks like and feels like. Design is how it works."

This is *the* operative aesthetic claim in the Jobs canon. It does two things at once:
- Rejects the marketing/styling conception of design.
- Bundles function, materials, micro-interactions, and unboxing into one indivisible artifact.

The implication for product decisions is structural: if "how it works" *is* design, then a product manager, engineer, and industrial designer cannot make independent decisions and converge at the end. They must be making the same decisions together, from the start.

## "The intersection of technology and the liberal arts"

From the iPad introduction, January 27, 2010, Yerba Buena Center for the Arts, San Francisco. Closing slide of the keynote — two street signs, one "Liberal Arts," one "Technology," meeting at an intersection. Jobs:

> "The reason that Apple is able to create products like the iPad is because we have always tried to be at the intersection of technology and liberal arts. To be able to make extremely advanced products from a technology point of view, but also have them be intuitive, easy to use, fun to use. So, that they really fit the users. The users don't have to come to them. They come to the user."

And:

> "It's in Apple's DNA that technology alone is not enough. It's technology married with the liberal arts, married with the humanities, that yield the results that make our hearts sing. And nowhere is that more true than in these post-PC devices."

The line operationalized in Apple's hiring (designers who studied calligraphy, photographers, musicians) and in the company's refusal to optimize purely for spec-sheet competitiveness. It is also the cultural source for Jobs' resistance to "engineering-driven" product roadmaps.

## "People don't know what they want until you show it to them"

Variant attributed to a 1998 BusinessWeek interview ("It's really hard to design products by focus groups. A lot of times, people don't know what they want until you show it to them") and reiterated in the Isaacson biography. The Henry Ford "faster horses" parable is the same idea older. Jobs' interpretation went one step further: he believed that *no amount* of customer research could substitute for a designer with taste making a confident call. Pixar's *Toy Story* development was the same principle applied to story.

The consequence for AI product strategy is non-trivial: Jobs would reject "build what users ask for in feedback surveys" as a product methodology. His framing is closer to "build what you'd want yourself, then make it good enough that users will recognize they wanted it once they see it."

## "Real artists ship"

Coined at the January 27-28, 1983 Macintosh team retreat at La Playa Hotel, Carmel. Documented in Frank Rose's *West of Eden* (1989) and Andy Hertzfeld's folklore.org. Jobs stood at the easel and unveiled three slogans for the Mac team. "Real artists ship" was one of them. The other two: "Mac in a book by 1986" and "It's not done until it ships."

The doctrine in one line: a finished and released product is the test of craft. Demo videos, prototypes, and internal-only versions are not art. The shipping deadline is what forces the tradeoffs that produce the final form.

## "Focus is about saying no"

From WWDC 1997, in answer to a question from the audience about Apple killing the OpenDoc project (the answer is sometimes paraphrased as "focusing is about saying no"):

> "People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas that there are. You have to pick carefully. I'm actually as proud of the things we haven't done as the things I have done. Innovation is saying no to a thousand things."

Operationalized when Jobs returned to Apple in summer 1997. From a then-350-item product line plus pipeline, he cut down to roughly ten core products within his first 90 days. The famous 2x2 matrix (consumer vs pro, desktop vs portable) was drawn at a product meeting after Jobs shouted "Stop! This is crazy" and went to the whiteboard. The four cells of the matrix became: iMac, iBook (Power Macintosh G3, PowerBook G3 for pro).

The first-year financial result: Apple swung from near-bankruptcy to a $309M profit in fiscal year 1998. The cuts also reduced headcount by approximately 3,000. The casualties included the Newton, the eMate, Cyberdog, OpenDoc, and the various Macintosh clone licenses.

## "Pirates, not Navy"

September 1982 Macintosh team retreat at Pajaro Dunes; reaffirmed at the January 1983 Carmel retreat. Jobs' line:

> "It's better to be a pirate than to join the Navy."

The Mac team flew a pirate flag (a Susan Kare design — Jolly Roger with the eyepatch replaced by the Apple rainbow logo) over Bandley 3, the Mac development building, after they moved in later in 1983. The framing was operational: the Mac team should be the fast, scrappy, rule-breaking insurgents *inside* Apple, free from the bureaucratic gravity that Jobs felt was suffocating the Apple II and Lisa divisions. The flag flew until the Mac shipped on January 24, 1984.

The principle generalizes: small teams with high autonomy and unreasonable ambition beat large teams with consensus processes. This shows up again at NeXT (a tiny team), at Pixar (a small studio competing against Disney), and at iPhone-era Apple (the iPhone team was sequestered inside Apple under Project Purple, on a separate floor with badge-restricted access).

## "A players hire A players; B players hire C players"

Jobs' hiring doctrine, most directly stated in the 1995 Cringely "Lost Interview." The reasoning: B players hire C players because they don't want anyone to outperform them, and once you let a B player in, the cascade is irreversible. The corollary doctrine: it is better to under-hire than to mis-hire.

This is operationally significant because it conflicts with growth-stage staffing conventions. Jobs would rather leave a role unfilled than fill it with a B player. The same doctrine applied to firings — he was famously willing to remove people he had personally hired the previous week if he concluded they were not A players.

## The 1995 Cringely Lost Interview — direct themes

Recorded for the 1996 PBS documentary *Triumph of the Nerds*. Only ten of seventy minutes were used in the documentary; the master tape was lost in shipping in 1995; VHS copies surfaced in the director's garage in October 2011 (the month Jobs died); released theatrically in 2012 as *Steve Jobs: The Lost Interview*.

Themes:
- **Process vs content**: large companies confuse the process by which past hits were made with the actual decisions. The process becomes the proxy for taste. Without ongoing taste, process produces increasingly diluted output.
- **The MBA problem**: people who don't know how products are actually made shouldn't be running product companies.
- **Why Microsoft made him sad in 1995**: not the market dominance, but the *aesthetic*. "They have absolutely no taste, and I don't mean that in a small way, I mean that in a big way."
- **The "death of innovation" trap**: a company stops innovating when sales and marketing people start running it, because they "have no idea about the product."

## The Hardware-Software-Service Trinity

Jobs' deepest strategic doctrine, never fully articulated in a single quote but visible across every product since the Apple I:
- **He refused to license the Mac OS** to other hardware makers, even when revenue pressure was acute. Sculley had partially reversed this with the 1994 Macintosh clone program; Jobs killed the clones within months of returning in 1997.
- **iPod + iTunes + iTunes Store** was the first three-layer integration: hardware + software + service.
- **iPhone + iOS + App Store** was the second, at greater scale.
- **iCloud, Apple Pay, Apple Music, Apple TV+, Apple Silicon** are all expressions of the same doctrine.

The framing for an AI product team: a model behind an API is the Microsoft-licensing equivalent. The Jobs instinct is to ship the model embedded inside a hardware/software/service stack so that the user experience is the artifact, not the model. The Apple Silicon Neural Engine + on-device Apple Intelligence approach is the post-Jobs Apple trying to apply the doctrine to AI, with mixed reception in 2024–2026.
