## Key themes

1. **Answer engine vs. search engine.** People want answers, not ten blue links. Search optimized for clicks and ad revenue is structurally misaligned with user intent. Citations are the trust mechanism that lets you ship LLM-generated answers without collapsing into hallucination.

2. **Curiosity is the moat.** Perplexity bets that the addressable market for *people trying to learn things* is much bigger than the addressable market for *people trying to navigate to a known page*. The "infinite value of knowledge" framing.

3. **The Google-as-incumbent thesis.** Google's business model (ads next to links) constrains how aggressively they can ship pure answer experiences. Perplexity's lack of legacy ad inventory is a feature, not a bug.

4. **RAG architecture.** Sourced answers require live retrieval plus citation-aware generation. The bottleneck is not the model — it's the retrieval substrate and the post-training that teaches the model to cite faithfully.

5. **Trillion-dollar question framing.** What is the question whose answer compounds the most knowledge? That's what an answer engine should optimize for, not query throughput.

6. **The lineage list.** Srinivas explicitly cites Larry Page, Sergey Brin, Jeff Bezos, Elon Musk, Jensen Huang, Mark Zuckerberg, and Yann LeCun as people whose moves he studies — a tell that he's modeling himself on the lineage of founders who reorganized markets.
