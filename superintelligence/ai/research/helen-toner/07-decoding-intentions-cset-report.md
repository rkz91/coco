## Report Details

**Full title:** "Decoding Intentions: Artificial Intelligence and Costly Signals"
**Published:** October 2023
**Authors:** Andrew Imbrie, Owen J. Daniels, Helen Toner
**Venue:** Center for Security and Emerging Technology (CSET) at Georgetown

## Core Concept

The report applies international relations theory — specifically the concept of "costly signals" — to AI strategy. Policymakers and companies signal their intentions through actions that would be costly to reverse. The question: how can intentions in AI be credibly revealed and assessed?

## Four Signaling Mechanisms Analyzed

1. **Tying hands** — public commitments that make backing down politically costly
2. **Sunk costs** — investments that are wasted if a commitment is abandoned
3. **Installment costs** — ongoing payments that signal sustained commitment
4. **Reducible costs** — investments that pay off only if the actor follows through

## The OpenAI vs. Anthropic Comparison (the part that triggered the board crisis)

The paper compared safety signaling by OpenAI and Anthropic:

- **OpenAI's ChatGPT release** (November 2022) was characterized as a potential "race to the bottom" trigger — a costly signal that **safety was not the binding constraint** on commercialization decisions.
- **Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy** was framed more favorably as a credible costly signal of safety commitment.

The OpenAI-side framing was the surface trigger for Altman's reported call to Toner warning that the paper "could cause problems" amid the FTC investigation. The board-level conflict had deeper roots, but the published paper was the proximate flashpoint.

## Why This Report Matters for Persona Synthesis

"Decoding Intentions" establishes Toner's intellectual framework on AI governance: she thinks in terms of **what actions would be costly to reverse** and therefore credible. Press releases, voluntary commitments, and safety claims that have no enforcement mechanism are not credible signals. This frame appears throughout her later writing:

- "Self-governance cannot withstand profit incentives" (May 2024 Economist) — because voluntary commitments are not costly
- "External auditing" with teeth (TED2024) — because audit findings impose costs that voluntary disclosure does not
- "Adaptation buffers" (April 2025 Substack) — because they invest in costly defensive infrastructure
- Distillation testimony (April 2026 Senate Judiciary) — because counter-misuse infrastructure is costly enough to be credible

The "costly signals" lens is the unifying mental model for her policy work.

## Andrew Imbrie

The senior co-author. CSET Senior Fellow and former US State Department official. Toner is the more public-facing co-author but the costly-signals framing draws on Imbrie's IR-theory background. Worth noting for context — the framework is not purely Toner's invention; it is the application of established IR theory to AI strategy.
