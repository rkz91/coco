## "How to Govern AI — Even If It's Hard to Predict"

**Venue:** TED2024, Vancouver.
**Approximate delivery:** April 2024.

## Central Thesis

The unpredictability of AI is not a reason to abandon governance. It is a reason to design governance that does not depend on accurate prediction.

Toner opens with the observation that experts themselves do not understand AI:

> "I don't understand AI, and neither does anyone else."

This is not abdication. It is the starting point. The right response is not to wait for clarity that will not arrive, but to design governance that is robust to uncertainty.

## Three Pillars of Her Framework

### 1. Measurement and Monitoring of AI Capabilities

Develop systematic methods to measure and monitor what AI systems can actually do — particularly on risk-relevant tasks (hacking critical infrastructure, manipulating political beliefs, biological design). Eval suites are the foundation. You cannot govern what you cannot measure.

### 2. Mandatory Disclosure Norms

Companies developing AI should be required to disclose detailed information about:

- What the systems can do (capability disclosures)
- What data they are trained on
- How risks are being identified and addressed
- What safety processes exist internally

This is the same critique she leveled at OpenAI privately as a board member — the company had "a small number of formal safety processes" and was not transparent about them, internally or externally. The TED talk generalizes the OpenAI-specific failure into a structural policy recommendation: disclosure should be mandatory, not discretionary.

### 3. Independent External Auditing — "Grading Their Own Homework"

The most-quoted framing from the talk. Toner argues that AI companies cannot be allowed to "grade their own homework" — internal safety evaluations conducted by the same company shipping the model cannot serve the public interest. She calls for an external auditing ecosystem with independent auditors empowered to assess safety, security, and ethical implications.

## Why This Talk Matters

The TED talk is the **distilled public articulation** of the policy framework she developed at CSET and tested as an OpenAI board member. The structure mirrors her later writing:

- TED2024 → "Decoding Intentions" (2023) precursor on credible signaling
- TED2024 → Economist op-ed (May 2024) on why self-governance fails
- TED2024 → "We're Arguing About AI Safety Wrong" (May 2025) on dynamist policy design
- TED2024 → "When AI Builds AI" (January 2026) on capability monitoring under automated R&D

The talk is also the cleanest evidence of her **voice style**: plain English, willing to say "I don't know," precise about what governance can and cannot achieve, allergic to grand civilizational rhetoric.
