## Essay 1: "Nonproliferation is the Wrong Approach to AI Misuse"

**Date:** April 5, 2025
**URL:** https://helentoner.substack.com/p/nonproliferation-is-the-wrong-approach

### Argument

Nuclear nonproliferation works because nuclear weapons don't get cheaper over time. AI misuse risks follow an inverse trajectory — frontier capabilities are expensive, but once a capability is achieved, replication becomes dramatically cheaper. Attempting indefinite nonproliferation of AI capabilities is "ineffective and freedom-damaging" because dangerous capabilities will inevitably proliferate within years.

### The "Adaptation Buffer" Alternative

> "We should think of the gap between frontier models and proliferated models as an adaptation buffer: a limited time window when we know what bad actors will soon be able to use AI for."

During that window, focus on:

- Cybersecurity capacity-building for critical infrastructure
- AI-assisted cyberdefense tool development
- Biosecurity monitoring, medical countermeasures, disease surveillance
- Defensive technology investment

### Scope

The essay addresses misuse risks only, not AI-takeover/control risks. It targets fixed-capability hazards (a specific bioweapon design becoming accessible), not the moving frontier itself.

## Essay 2: "'Long' Timelines to Advanced AI Have Gotten Crazy Short"

**Date:** April 1, 2025
**URL:** https://helentoner.substack.com/p/long-timelines-to-advanced-ai-have

### Argument

Expert consensus on AI timelines has compressed dramatically. What was once a "bold claim" is now mainstream — even publicly cautious researchers (LeCun, Narayanan) assign material probability mass to transformative AI within 10-20 years. The debate hasn't lost its participants; it has compressed its calendar.

### Implication

Policymakers and institutions should plan as if transformative AI is a 10-20 year horizon, not a 50-100 year horizon. Governance design needs to absorb that compression.

## Essay 3: "In Search of a Dynamist Vision for Safe Superhuman AI"

**Date:** May 12, 2025 (cross-posted as "We're Arguing About AI Safety Wrong" at AI Frontiers)
**URLs:** https://helentoner.substack.com/p/dynamism-vs-stasis ; https://ai-frontiers.org/articles/were-arguing-about-ai-safety-wrong

### Argument

Toner applies Virginia Postrel's **dynamism vs. stasism** dichotomy to the AI safety debate:

- **Dynamism:** "a world of constant creation, discovery, and competition"
- **Stasism:** "a regulated, engineered world... that values stability and control"

Her acknowledgement is important: the AI safety community **does** exhibit stasist tendencies. The preference for fewer leading AI projects, nonproliferation framings, 2023 licensing proposals for frontier model training, and overemphasis on predetermined "theories of victory" — these are stasist patterns. She is critical of them despite agreeing on the underlying risks.

### Direct Quotes

> "The AI safety community... can have a stasist vibe."

> "Stasists seek specifics to govern each new situation and keep things under control."

> "I hope for a dynamic and inherently unstable future of emergent, complex messiness."

### Her Preferred Dynamist Policy Instruments

- Transparency and whistleblower protections that empower individual action
- Third-party audit ecosystems that create enforceable commitments without centralizing
- Open-source AI development to keep capability distributed
- Differential technological development — building defensive technologies ahead of offensive ones

## Essay 4: "Taking Jaggedness Seriously"

**Date:** November 24, 2025
**URL:** https://helentoner.substack.com/p/taking-jaggedness-seriously

### Argument

AI capabilities are persistently uneven across domains and will remain so. Models that solve PhD-level physics problems still fail at counting line intersections in a drawing. "Jaggedness" is not a transitional state that smooths out as scale increases — it is structural, driven by:

1. Differential ease of verifying success across domains
2. Differential context window demands
3. Differential adversarial robustness requirements
4. The cognitive vs. physical task boundary

### Direct Quote

> "Maybe AI will keep getting better _and_ maybe AI will keep sucking in important ways."

### Implication

Policy should focus on "transition turbulence" — the actual order, speed, and intensity with which specific capabilities develop — rather than on smooth-curve forecasts.

## Essay 5: "Personalized AI Is Rerunning the Worst Part of Social Media's Playbook"

**Date:** 2025 (precise date unverified at time of synthesis; URL: https://helentoner.substack.com/p/personalized-ai-social-media-playbook)

### Argument

Personalization of AI assistants is recreating the engagement-maximization dynamics that produced the worst outcomes of social media — addictive product design, attention capture, opaque optimization targets. Policymakers should treat this as a known failure mode, not a novel question.

## CSET Publication: "When AI Builds AI"

**Date:** January 2026
**URL:** https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/when-ai-builds-ai/
**Co-authors:** Helen Toner, Kendrea Beers, Steve Newman, Saif Khan, and others

### Headline Finding

> "Increasingly automated AI R&D is a potential source of major strategic surprise, with scenarios possible in which AI R&D becomes highly automated, the pace accelerates dramatically, and the resulting systems pose extreme risks."

### Methodology

Synthesizes a CSET-hosted expert workshop from July 2025. Documents that frontier labs are already using internal AI systems to accelerate AI R&D before public release of those systems.

### Why This Matters

This is the **policy artifact** that operationalizes Toner's "we need better measurement and monitoring" pillar from the TED talk. If AI R&D itself is being automated, then the public's ability to track capability progress depends entirely on disclosure norms that currently do not exist.

## Pattern Across All Five Essays

Toner's 2025-2026 writing converges on a single policy program:

1. **Measurement** of capabilities (not just benchmarks — risk-relevant evals)
2. **Disclosure** of what labs know about their own models
3. **External auditing** with teeth
4. **Defensive infrastructure** investment during the adaptation-buffer window
5. **Dynamist** (not stasist) policy design — decentralized, transparent, empowering individual action

This is the coherent policy framework that the persona's `public_stances` and `mental_models` fields should reflect.
