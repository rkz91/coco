## The Sequence of Events

### Late 2021: Joining the Board

Toner joined OpenAI's board in late 2021 at the invitation of Holden Karnofsky (Open Philanthropy). The board structure at the time made directors fiduciaries of the OpenAI nonprofit and accountable to its altruistic mission — not to shareholders — despite Microsoft's investment.

### October 2023: "Decoding Intentions"

Toner co-authored "Decoding Intentions: Artificial Intelligence and Costly Signals" with Andrew Imbrie and a Georgetown fellow at CSET, published October 2023. The paper critiqued OpenAI's safety and ethics practices around ChatGPT and GPT-4 — including copyright concerns, labor conditions for data annotators, and the model's susceptibility to jailbreaks — while comparing OpenAI's approach unfavorably to Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy.

Altman reportedly called Toner directly, told her the paper "could cause problems" amid an active FTC investigation into OpenAI's data practices, and (per Toner's later account) began lying to other board members in order to push her off the board.

### November 17, 2023: The Vote

Four board members — Helen Toner, Tasha McCauley, Ilya Sutskever, and Adam D'Angelo — voted to remove Altman as CEO. The board's stated reason in the public statement: Altman was "not consistently candid in his communications with the board, hindering its ability to exercise its responsibilities."

### November 21, 2023: The Resolution

Four days after the firing, Altman was reinstated as CEO. Toner, McCauley, and Sutskever departed the board, replaced by former Treasury Secretary Lawrence Summers and Bret Taylor. D'Angelo remained.

## Toner's Specific Allegations (May 2024 TED AI Show)

In her first long-form interview after the OpenAI internal investigation concluded, on Bilawal Sidhu's TED AI Show (May 28, 2024), Toner laid out the specific allegations:

### On Pattern of Dishonesty

> "For years, Sam had made it very difficult for the board to actually do that job by withholding information, misrepresenting things that were happening at the company, in some cases outright lying to the board."

### On Safety Processes — the Canonical Quote

> Altman gave the board "inaccurate information about the small number of formal safety processes that the company did have in place" on multiple occasions.

### On ChatGPT Launch

> "We learned about ChatGPT on Twitter."

The board was not informed in advance about the launch of ChatGPT in November 2022.

### On OpenAI Startup Fund

Altman had not informed the board that he personally owned the OpenAI Startup Fund — a venture capital vehicle over which he made management decisions — despite claiming to be an independent board member with no financial interest in the company.

### On Psychological Abuse Reports

Two OpenAI executives had reported to the board patterns of "psychological abuse" from Altman, supported by screenshots and documentation of "lying and being manipulative in different situations."

### On Why the Board Could Not Continue

> "Sam could always come up with some kind of innocuous-sounding explanation of why it wasn't a big deal, or it was misinterpreted or whatever."

> "All four of us who fired him came to the conclusion that we just couldn't believe things that Sam was telling us, and that's a completely unworkable place to be in as a board."

### On Retaliation

> "After the paper came out, Sam started lying to other board members in order to try and push me off the board."

## The May 26, 2024 Economist Op-Ed (Toner + McCauley)

Co-authored with Tasha McCauley. The single most important post-crisis policy artifact. Title: "AI firms mustn't govern themselves, say ex-members of OpenAI's board."

### Key Quotes

> "Based on our experience, we believe that self-governance cannot reliably withstand the pressure of profit incentives."

> "Even with the best of intentions, without external oversight, this kind of self-regulation will end up unenforceable."

The op-ed accused Altman of cultivating "a toxic culture of lying" and called for government intervention, "effective regulatory frameworks now," and vigilance against regulatory capture.

## OpenAI's Response

Brett Taylor (OpenAI board chair after the crisis) issued a statement saying the board was "disappointed that Ms. Toner continues to revisit these issues" and emphasized that the independent WilmerHale investigation "concluded that the prior board's decision was not based on concerns regarding product safety or security."

The substantive truth of Toner's allegations has not been adjudicated. The investigation focused on the board's process, not on the truth of specific claims about Altman's conduct.

## Why This Matters for Persona Synthesis

The November 2023 board crisis is the canonical case study Toner cites in every subsequent piece of public commentary on AI governance. Her policy framing — that lab self-governance has structurally failed, that the CEO-as-employee dynamic prevents safety culture from working, that independent external oversight is necessary — is grounded in her direct experience as a fiduciary director who was told one thing and learned another.

For the persona file, this is the **central productive_conflict** with Sam Altman, and it is the **lived evidence** behind her public_stances on lab governance.
