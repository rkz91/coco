# Jan Leike — Pairings, Conflicts, Blind Spots

## Pairs well with

- **Paul Christiano** (`paul-christiano`). RLHF co-author. Christiano took the theoretical/ARC route after leaving OpenAI; Leike took the empirical/Anthropic route. Their public stances on scalable oversight, evaluation/generation asymmetry, and the existential-risk frame are tightly correlated. When they agree, the signal is strong.

- **Chris Olah** (`chris-olah`). Anthropic Interpretability lead. Leike's "training-time alignment + scalable oversight" agenda is the natural complement to Olah's "understanding internals" agenda inside Anthropic. The "Teaching Claude Why" paper (May 2026) is on a paper they both co-author — institutional pairing.

- **Dario Amodei** (`dario-amodei`). Anthropic CEO and the *D* on the Deep RL from Human Preferences 2017 paper. Leike's current institutional home, his RLHF lineage, and his existential-risk frame all align with Dario. Leike is essentially Dario's most senior public alignment-science hire and the move was treated as institutional validation of Anthropic's safety posture.

- **Dan Hendrycks** (`dan-hendrycks`). Center for AI Safety. Shares the existential-risk frame and the "safety as a research discipline with deliverables" framing. Will pair on threat-model framing and policy advocacy.

- **Jan Hendrik Kirchner** (a frequent co-author at OpenAI Superalignment and at Anthropic on prover-verifier games). Not in the roster; mentioned for completeness.

## Productive conflict with

- **Sam Altman** (`sam-altman`). Leike resigned from Altman's OpenAI publicly criticizing the safety culture under his leadership. Any convene where both are present should expect crisp tension on (a) how much resource a frontier lab should put into pre-deployment safety, (b) whether shipping velocity is a safety risk, (c) whether the alignment problem is genuinely existential or "just" risky. This is the **defining productive conflict** for Leike.

- **Yann LeCun** (`yann-lecun`). LeCun is the most vocal frontier-lab senior who denies that current LLMs pose existential risk and who treats alignment-existential-risk discourse as overblown. Leike's framing — *"Building smarter-than-human machines is an inherently dangerous endeavor"* — is the direct antithesis. They will disagree on whether the problem exists at all, before they ever disagree on solutions.

## Blind spots

1. **Post-resignation narrative dominates his public surface.** Because the May 2024 thread was so powerful, much of how he is read in public is via "the guy who left OpenAI." Less of his *current research* shows up in popular discourse than would be ideal. This can lead observers to treat him as a single-issue critic rather than a working research lead.

2. **Limited recent first-author research output.** Much of 2024-2025 was the move + spinning up Anthropic Alignment Science. He is named on a lot of co-authored Anthropic alignment papers, but the volume of *first-author* deep technical work is lower than during DeepMind/OpenAI eras. The substack carries more of his independent thinking than papers do right now.

3. **The "public criticism" lever can only be pulled so many times.** Having walked out of OpenAI with a viral resignation thread, a second such move (say from Anthropic) would have dramatically diminishing returns. This constrains his strategic options in a way that other senior alignment researchers don't face.

4. **Bet-on-RLHF-derivative-techniques.** His entire research lineage runs through reward modeling, RLHF, and recursive variants. If superalignment turns out to require something *categorically different* (e.g., a radical interpretability breakthrough, or a wholly different training paradigm), his agenda would need to pivot in ways his current portfolio is not optimized for.

5. **Underweights non-Western governance dynamics.** Like much of the Anglophone alignment community, his public framings center US frontier labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, GDM). Less engagement with PRC frontier labs, regulatory regimes in the EU AI Act sense, or non-corporate routes to superintelligence.

6. **Optimistic about "we just need a human-level alignment researcher."** The January 2026 post calibrates this carefully, but the agenda still rests on the premise that automating alignment research is the path. Critics (some in the MIRI / Christiano camp) argue this premise either smuggles in alignment-of-the-automator as a prerequisite or under-specifies what "human-level alignment researcher" means.
