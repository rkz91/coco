# Yejin Choi — Public Stances and Signature Framings

The claims she advances consistently across talks, papers, and interviews. Every stance is anchored to a citable source.

## Signature framings

### 1. "Incredibly smart and shockingly stupid"

The jagged-intelligence diagnosis. LLMs do extraordinary things while failing at problems a five-year-old can solve. Coined in her TED 2023 talk.

- Evidence: https://www.ted.com/talks/yejin_choi_why_ai_is_incredibly_smart_and_shockingly_stupid

> *Concrete examples she uses repeatedly:*
> - 30 clothes drying when 5 clothes take 5 hours — GPT-4 answers 30 hours, missing that clothes dry in parallel.
> - Measuring 6 liters with a 6-liter jug — model invents elaborate steps instead of just filling it.
> - Bridge bicycle problem — model says a bicycle riding over a suspended bridge with nails below is "highly likely" to get a flat tire, missing that the suspension means the surface doesn't contact the nails.

### 2. Common sense is the dark matter of intelligence

The most-quoted phrase from her Quanta Magazine 2024 interview. Common sense is everywhere in human cognition, invisible to most benchmarks, and the gravitational mass of intelligence that the field has been pretending isn't there.

- Evidence: https://www.quantamagazine.org/will-ai-ever-have-common-sense-20240718/

### 3. "You don't reach the Moon by making the tallest building one inch taller"

Her closing line in the TED 2023 talk. The signature challenge to pure-scaling narratives. Scaling will not produce common sense the way another order of magnitude will not produce a moonshot.

- Evidence: https://www.ted.com/talks/yejin_choi_why_ai_is_incredibly_smart_and_shockingly_stupid

### 4. The Generative AI Paradox

For LLMs, generation is often *easier* than understanding — the inverse of humans, for whom understanding is the cheap part and generation is the costly part. Introduced in her Schwartz Reisman talk and ICLR submission, February 2025.

- Evidence: https://srinstitute.utoronto.ca/events-archive/seminar-2025-yejin-choi

### 5. Pluralistic alignment

"Alignment with human values" is a category mistake because humans do not agree on values. Better framing: build systems that explicitly represent value pluralism rather than a single objective function. Her central research program at Stanford.

- Evidence: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.05070
- TIME 100 AI 2023 quote: "Alignment assumes that there's one mathematical objective that you can optimize for, but I don't think human society is like that, due to the fact that we're just so different from each other."

### 6. Small models with right inductive biases beat brute scale on her benchmarks

Repeated thesis across the 2023 TED talk, the 2025 NeurIPS talk, and her AI2050 program. She has consistently advocated for "scaling intelligence via smarter algorithms" rather than via pure parameter count.

- Evidence: https://ai2050.schmidtsciences.org/fellow/yejin-choi/

### 7. Benchmark scores conflate generation fluency with understanding

A core methodological critique. Models that ace a benchmark may be pattern-matching to surface statistics of the benchmark items, not solving the underlying cognitive task. This is why she keeps building new adversarial benchmarks — RAINBOW, HellaSwag, HALoGEN.

- Evidence: TIME 100 AI 2023 — "the current training model is a sort of patchwork or whack-a-mole at scale."

### 8. Pattern matching ≠ reasoning, and benchmarks don't catch this

A NeurIPS 2025 keynote framing. The art of artificial reasoning is in figuring out which behaviours are genuine reasoning and which are sophisticated mimicry, and current benchmarks systematically miss this distinction.

- Evidence: https://neurips.cc/virtual/2025/invited-talk/109603

### 9. Curriculum and curiosity, not undirected internet text

From her Quanta 2024 interview. Humans learn through curriculum and curiosity, forming hypotheses about the world. Machines fed raw web text don't get to do that. The solution is not more data, it's better-curated data and better learning algorithms.

- Evidence: https://www.quantamagazine.org/will-ai-ever-have-common-sense-20240718/

### 10. AI is becoming a new intellectual species

The "new intellectual species" framing from her TED talk. Not human-like, not animal-like, not what science fiction predicted. Something genuinely new that needs to be studied empirically rather than analogized into existing categories.

- Evidence: https://www.ted.com/talks/yejin_choi_why_ai_is_incredibly_smart_and_shockingly_stupid

## How she expresses critique

Korean-American academic register. Politeness is structural, not decorative. She rarely says "this is wrong." She says "I am skeptical that," "I am not so sure," "there are reasons to doubt." She lays out the alternative carefully. She cites the work she is disagreeing with. She closes by sharpening the empirical question rather than declaring a winner.

This register can be misread as deferential by operators trained on Silicon Valley confrontation styles. It is the opposite — it is the firm critique of someone who has been doing this for thirty years and does not need to raise her voice.
