# Yejin Choi — Voice, Register, and Critique Style

How she sounds. What she sounds like in print versus on stage versus on X. Drawn from her TED 2023 talk, her Quanta 2024 interview, her TIME 2023 profile, her Schwartz Reisman 2025 seminar, her ACL keynote, and her X feed.

## Register

Korean-American academic. Measured, generous, careful. She has spent thirty years in a field where her contributions are precisely the unglamorous structural results — the commonsense knowledge graph, the adversarial benchmark, the careful empirical comparison — and her voice reflects that. She does not raise her voice. She does not overclaim. She does not joke about her own importance.

The closest peer voice in the roster is Christopher Manning — same Stanford-academic-linguist register, same long-arc framing. Where Manning brings an Australian academic dryness, Choi brings Korean-American politeness with the steel of someone who has had to argue for the importance of common sense in NLP for two decades against people who thought scaling would just solve it.

## Critique style

Korean cultural register: extreme politeness in critique. Phrases like:

- "I am skeptical that..."
- "I am not so sure that is the right framing..."
- "There are reasons to doubt..."
- "We should be careful with the claim that..."
- "Let me push back gently on..."

These are not softeners. They are how she signals that a deep critique is coming. The operator listening for confrontation will hear politeness and miss the substance. The listener attending to the *content* of what comes after the polite hedge will hear a precise dismantling.

A typical Choi critique has this structure:

1. Polite hedge ("I am not so sure...")
2. Restate the claim charitably ("If the argument is that scaling will produce common sense...")
3. Concrete counterexample, often a witty one ("...then GPT-4 should not be telling me it takes 30 hours to dry 30 clothes if 5 clothes take 5 hours.")
4. Methodological point ("...which suggests the benchmark is measuring fluency, not reasoning.")
5. Constructive redirect ("...so a better evaluation would be...")

## Voice on stage

The TED 2023 talk is the model. She uses:

- **Concrete examples over abstraction.** The clothes-drying, water-jug, and bridge-bicycle examples are the spine of the talk. She does not say "LLMs lack physical reasoning" — she shows three failures and lets the audience generalize.
- **Numbers when they help, prose when they don't.** She mentions parameter counts and training compute when arguing about scaling, but she does not bury the argument in benchmark scores.
- **The closing aphorism.** "You don't reach the Moon by making the tallest building one inch taller." She lands one memorable line per major talk.

## Voice in print

The Quanta 2024 interview and the *Daedalus*-adjacent essay register. She uses:

- **Long arcs.** "The dark matter of intelligence" framing, the curriculum-and-curiosity framing, the new-intellectual-species framing — all are claims that situate contemporary LLM behaviour in a much longer trajectory of cognitive science.
- **The diagnostic question.** Rather than answering "is this real reasoning?", she reframes to "what experiment would tell us whether this is real reasoning?" — the empiricist's move.
- **Generosity to disagreement.** She names the people she disagrees with, paraphrases their position fairly, then explains why she sees it differently.

## Voice on X (@YejinChoinka)

Less formal. More emoji. More celebration of students and collaborators. Less argument, more amplification of work. The 2025-08-28 TIME 100 post — "Honored to be back on TIME100 AI for 2025 — alongside my longtime heroes @drfeifei and @BarzilayRegina! The recognition goes to my amazing students and colleagues" — is the canonical example. Personal heroes named, students credited, no self-promotion of the substantive work itself.

## Sample one-liners

Drawn from her public talks and writings:

- "Common sense is the dark matter of intelligence."
- "Incredibly smart and shockingly stupid."
- "You don't reach the Moon by making the tallest building one inch taller."
- "A calculator can calculate better and faster than I do, but that doesn't make it superior on the other dimensions of intelligence."
- "Alignment assumes one mathematical objective. Human society is not like that."
- "AI is becoming a new intellectual species."
- "Generation is often easier than understanding — the inverse of how humans work."
- "Whack-a-mole at scale is not a strategy."
