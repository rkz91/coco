# Key Quotes and Public Stances

Compiled from 80,000 Hours episode 217 (`https://80000hours.org/podcast/episodes/beth-barnes-ai-safety-evals/`, June 2, 2025); AXRP Episode 34 (`https://axrp.net/episode/2024/07/28/episode-34-ai-evaluations-beth-barnes.html`); the EA Forum summary thread (`https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/QFQdW3iXnT7SHCm9y/`); and Beth Barnes's X account (`https://x.com/BethMayBarnes`).

Each quote is annotated with the source so the persona profile can cite cleanly.

## On the seven-month rule

> "Over that time there's a pretty good fit to a doubling of something like every six months."
— 80,000 Hours #217, June 2, 2025. (The longer-run figure she cites elsewhere is seven months; she uses six months when describing the more recent acceleration.)

> "AI models today have a 50% chance of successfully completing a task that would take an expert human an hour. Seven months ago, that number was roughly 30 minutes — and seven months before that, 15 minutes."
— 80,000 Hours #217.

## On urgency and expert posture

> "It seems hard to rule out even shorter [timelines]. Is there 1% chance of six, nine months? Yeah, that seems pretty plausible."
— 80,000 Hours #217.

> "The experts are not on top of this. And to the extent that I am an expert, I am an expert telling you you should freak out."
— 80,000 Hours #217.

> "I stand by my claims here that the world is not on track to keep risk from AI to an acceptable level, and we desperately need more people working on these problems."
— Beth Barnes on X, `https://x.com/BethMayBarnes/status/1930091384213778743`, June 4, 2025.

## On pre-deployment evaluation

> "Pre-deployment evals could actually be bad, because delaying deployment could be bad if that means you miss the window to sound the alarm."
— 80,000 Hours #217.

> "Ideally, before you start training a model and before you commit a huge amount of expensive compute to producing this artefact, you look at the most capable models you have already."
— 80,000 Hours #217.

## On lab self-evaluation

> "I haven't seen that much evidence that there are these really good benchmarks and they're just inside labs. That may be true, but I don't particularly have reason to believe that labs are super on top of this either."
— AXRP Episode 34, July 28, 2024.

> "Labs more want a 'Can you promise that you will run an eval on all of our models when we want to?' sort of thing... that's not what we're excited about."
— 80,000 Hours #217.

## On AI scheming and chain-of-thought

> "Models could reason through that in the chain of thought, saying 'I need to pretend to not be able to do this,' and you would never know."
— 80,000 Hours #217.

> "We're really not very good at reasoning, except for out loud in English... but I think we're maybe heading for a world where that's not true anymore."
— 80,000 Hours #217.

> "We're maybe heading for a world where [models aren't] really very good at reasoning except for out loud in English."
— 80,000 Hours #217 (variant of the above, both phrasings circulating in the EA Forum write-up).

## On METR's competence

> "I think METR has the technical competence... our evals and elicitation are better than stuff any lab has published, basically."
— 80,000 Hours #217.

## On methodology

> "We've had humans attempt basically all of these tasks, and we record how long it takes... how long does it take them?"
— 80,000 Hours #217.

> "If you don't have fine-tuning access… you could be getting a wildly different result than you should be."
— AXRP Episode 34.

## On the GPT-5 evaluation

> "The good news: due to increased access (plus improved evals science) we were able to do a more meaningful evaluation than with past models, and we think we have substantial evidence that this model does not pose a catastrophic risk via autonomy / loss of control threat models."
— Beth Barnes on X, `https://x.com/BethMayBarnes/status/1953532074809602072`, August 7, 2025.

## On open-weight models (reversal)

The 80,000 Hours episode documents that Barnes has reversed her earlier position on open-source releases. She now views open-weight models as net beneficial for safety research because they reduce unhealthy lab secrecy and allow third parties to run the evaluations that labs do not run themselves. The strongest available signal that she weights independent-evaluation access higher than the conventional "open weights are dangerous" framing.

## On frontier company employment

The EA Forum summary notes she disputes the effectiveness of safety-focused employees attempting to change company culture through internal influence. She favors external research independence over inside-the-tent advocacy. This is consistent with her own career arc — leaving OpenAI in 2022 explicitly to be an independent voice.

## On the catastrophic-risk threshold

The implicit threshold she works from, derived from the METR GPT-5 report (August 7, 2025): "the 10X researcher acceleration threshold... requires a 50%-time horizon of at least a week (40 hours)." This 40-hour figure is METR's operating definition of when a frontier model has crossed into the AI-R&D-self-improvement risk regime.

## On the elicitation gap and Goodhart's law

> "If there is some prompt that causes a model to perform well on a wide range of tasks, that prompt could be used to create a capable agent. However, task-specific elicitation that's much more narrowly targeted than the distribution of tasks covering the real threat model requires caution."
— METR autonomy-evals guide, `https://metr.github.io/autonomy-evals-guide/elicitation-gap/`.
