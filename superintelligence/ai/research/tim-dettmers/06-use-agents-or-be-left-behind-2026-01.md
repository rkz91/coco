# Tim Dettmers — "Use Agents or Be Left Behind: A Personal Guide to Automating Your Own Work" (2026-01-13)

Source: https://timdettmers.com/2026/01/13/use-agents-or-be-left-behind/

This post is the personal-practice companion to SERA. It's how Dettmers actually uses coding agents day-to-day after eight months of Claude Code, and it sharpens the persona's voice on workflow, automation, and accessibility.

## Headline claim

> "More than 90% of code and text should be written by agents."

Hyperbolic on its face, but he frames it as based on eight months of measured Claude Code use, not aspiration.

## What works

- **Blog posts.** Workflow: speak content aloud (~1h) → agent processes (~10min) → human edits (~2h). "95% of the text from both blog posts comes from an AI model" while still preserving personal voice through selective editing.
- **Grant proposals.** A sentence-by-sentence structural template extracted from successful proposals, applied by an agent. Four-page grant: ~1.5 hours.
- **Meta-reviews for conferences.** Agents analyze reviewer discussions, surface disagreements, summarize borderline cases.

## The factory-automation framing

He draws explicitly from manufacturing automation thinking:

> "If you take your current degree of automation and increase it by a new technology, then you improve by a certain percentage."

He distinguishes short-term ROI ("did this task get faster?") from long-term "automation muscles" — building the workflow skill even when the immediate payoff is marginal.

## What didn't work: email

After 100+ hours building a Vim-optimized email automation pipeline:

> "Just using Gmail is faster."

The interface-navigation overhead negated efficiency gains even with good categorization and draft generation. This is a useful concrete counterexample — Dettmers does not pretend everything automates.

## Accessibility

He built a voice tool because he has **carpal tunnel in both hands** and finds voice interaction "much more comfortable than typing" while allowing parallel task management. Accessibility-as-research-driver is not abstract for him.

## Student tools

He has built:
- A Connected Papers replication (successful algorithm, the post calls the UX a failure).
- Frontier-model API access "at 1% of the usual API costs" for his students.
- Custom research infrastructure deliberately avoiding what he describes as biases in commercial platforms.

The 1%-of-API-cost provisioning is consistent with the bitsandbytes / QLoRA "consumer-GPU finetuning" arc: same accessibility instinct, applied to API access rather than memory.

## Reality check on job displacement

He argues skilled workers level up rather than get replaced. Resources are finite, so directing agent labor is persistently valuable. Light pushback against the "AGI takes all jobs" framing in his own field.

## Why this matters for the persona

- Concrete behavioral evidence to back the "accessibility is a moral and epistemic priority" stance.
- Shows the voice — practical, willing to admit when an automation didn't work, grounded in measurable workflow steps.
- Provides a "what he uses agents for" answer that's more specific than the SERA post.
