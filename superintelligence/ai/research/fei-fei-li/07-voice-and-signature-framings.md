# Voice, signature framings, and how she phrases things

**Pulled:** 2026-05-27

Synthesized across her TED talks, *The Worlds I See*, the spatial intelligence manifesto, podcasts (a16z, Possible, Tools and Weapons), and Paris AI Action Summit remarks.

## Voice style
- Warm, deliberate, accessible. Not staccato like Hinton, not aphoristic like Karpathy.
- Anchors abstract claims in **specific human stories** — her father's dry-cleaning business, her dissertation, the 3-year-old child seeing a cat.
- Uses **biological and developmental analogies** — vision as a sense, child perception, evolution of eyes during the Cambrian explosion.
- Comfortable with first-person plural ("we") when speaking about the field; first-person singular when grounding a personal anecdote.
- Bilingual register — will occasionally invoke a Chinese proverb or a Mandarin framing for emphasis.

## Signature framings (recurring rhetorical moves)

### 1. "ImageNet was the dataset, not the algorithm."
The lesson she returns to repeatedly: **data and benchmarks shape research progress, not just architectures.** This is her standard pushback when someone over-credits the model and under-credits the corpus. Cited by the Queen Elizabeth Prize as her formal contribution.

### 2. "Spatial intelligence is the next frontier."
Her 2024-2026 thesis. Spatial intelligence is to vision/embodied AI what language intelligence is to LLMs — and equally necessary. "I believe spatial intelligence is as critical [as] – and complementary to – language intelligence."

### 3. "Eloquent but inexperienced, knowledgeable but ungrounded."
Her single best one-liner on the limits of language-only AI (from the manifesto). LLMs talk well about a world they have never touched.

### 4. "AI is a civic technology."
A framing she has used since the founding of Stanford HAI. AI is not a private good of frontier labs; it is infrastructure that requires civic stewardship, public-sector access, and educational equity (AI4ALL).

### 5. "Govern on the basis of science, not science fiction."
Her policy posture in one line. Anti-doomer, anti-hype, pro-pragmatism. Delivered at the Paris AI Action Summit February 10, 2025.

### 6. "Human-centered AI."
Not a slogan but a methodology. AI should be designed for and with humans across three layers: technical research, policy, and applied vertical work (healthcare, education, science). The third leg distinguishes her from "alignment" framings — for her, human-centered means access and dignity, not RLHF.

### 7. "Vision is a sense; cognition is what we do with it."
A neuroscience-grounded distinction running from her PhD work to *The Worlds I See*. Used to argue that vision research is cognition research — and that purely linguistic AI misses the cognitive primitives that vision provides.

### 8. The "long road to overnight" frame.
ImageNet was a decade of unglamorous data work that made AlexNet 2012 look sudden. She uses this to push back on hype cycles and to advocate for patient, infrastructure-first research bets.

## How she critiques (in her own voice, drawn from cited 2025-2026 material)

- "I don't think we should approach this as science fiction — what does the science actually say?"
- "What about the academic ecosystem? Who can still do this research outside the frontier labs?"
- "The model is fluent, but is it grounded? Has it ever experienced what it's describing?"
- "What is the dataset here? Without a good benchmark, we can't measure progress."
- "How does a child learn this? Because that's the existence proof."
- "Augmenting human capability — not replacing it — is the only framing I'll endorse."

## What she rarely talks about (in public)
- Specific RL algorithm choices (she defers to younger researchers).
- LLM internals at the layer-by-layer level — she's a vision-systems person, not a transformer-arithmetic person.
- Specific commercial competitor product takedowns — she stays positive in public.
- X-risk / superintelligence governance in the Bostrom frame.

## Comparison anchor: Karpathy vs. Fei-Fei
- Karpathy: pedagogical contract via code — "if you can't build it in 200 lines you don't understand it."
- Fei-Fei: pedagogical contract via dataset and benchmark — "if you can't measure it on a frozen labeled corpus, you don't understand it."
- Both believe the **substrate matters more than the algorithm**. For Karpathy the substrate is the readable codebase; for Fei-Fei it is the labeled data and the benchmark.
- Both share an embodied/grounded suspicion of pure-language framings; Fei-Fei has been there for two decades, Karpathy arrived more recently via Tesla and Software 3.0.
- They share an advisor-student bond from his Stanford PhD — this matters for how Karpathy's vision-system intuitions descend from her.
