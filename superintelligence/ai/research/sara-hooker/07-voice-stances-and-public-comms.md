## Voice register

Warm, plainspoken, and pointed. The TIME 100 profile describes her as having a "warm disposition" but being "often grumpy" about the field, which lines up with the public-comms pattern: she opens warm, then makes a sharp structural critique, then closes with an invitation.

Frequent verbal moves:

- "Important problems await." She uses problem-quality as the lever for talent and direction.
- "The next decade will be shaped by..." She frames in decade-units, not quarter-units.
- "All intelligence adapts, so should AI." (sarahooker.me tagline.) She prefers biological/evolutionary analogies for adaptive systems, paired with sharp economic claims for compute access.
- She names her opponents structurally, not personally — "scaling-pilled approaches," "frontier labs serving the same models to everyone at high cost," "FLOPs as a governance instrument" — rather than naming individual labs or CEOs.

## Recurring signature framings

1. **Hardware lottery determines which research wins.** The foundational claim, from her 2020 essay. Everything else is downstream.
2. **Efficiency is the inclusion variable.** If frontier models cost $X per query, they are inaccessible to anyone whose budget is < $X — which is most of the world. Efficiency work is therefore the substrate of global-majority AI access.
3. **Multilingual AI is morally and commercially necessary.** Moral case: serving 100 languages serves the global majority. Commercial case: an enterprise vendor whose product works in 100 languages wins multinational customers that English-only vendors lose. She refuses to treat these as in tension.
4. **Open weights are necessary but not sufficient.** Open-weights releases do not equalize access if compute is the gate. (Implicit throughout her Cohere For AI work and her support for the Command R7B open weights release in December 2024.)
5. **The global majority should be in the loop.** Aya's 3,000-researcher / 119-country participation is the proof point.
6. **Pruning, sparsity, and adaptation have been under-sold.** Top-line accuracy hides the disparate-impact cost of compression. Disaggregated evaluation is the only honest reporting standard.
7. **We measure progress wrong.** This is the meta-claim. Scaling laws, leaderboards, English-average accuracy, FLOP count — all of these are aggregate metrics that hide who is being served and who is being failed.
8. **Adaptation efficiency is the next-decade variable.** The Adaption Labs thesis, made explicit in the TechCrunch piece and her January 2026 tweet.

## How she handles disagreement

In MLST episode #92 and the July 2024 follow-up she models a particular style of disagreement: she names the strongest version of her opponent's claim before she critiques it. On compute thresholds, she first concedes that there is a legitimate governance problem and that something has to be regulated, and only then makes the case that FLOPs is the wrong instrument. This is consistent with her practice of structural rather than personal critique.

## Things she rarely says

- She does not engage much with AGI timelines per se. When asked, she redirects to capability deficits (multilingual coverage, real-world adaptation, long-tail robustness) rather than calendar predictions.
- She rarely talks about alignment in the Anthropic/MIRI register. Her safety frame is disparate-impact and access, not deception or corrigibility.
- She does not publicly attack named individuals at competitor labs. The named opposition is structural ("closed frontier labs," "scaling-pilled approaches").

## LinkedIn / long-form voice

The "Adaptable Intelligence" LinkedIn essay (October 2025) is her clearest long-form articulation of the Adaption Labs thesis in her own writing. It frames adaptation as the natural successor to scaling, places the move in the lineage of evolutionary biology, and explicitly does not promise an AGI breakthrough — she promises efficiency and access.
