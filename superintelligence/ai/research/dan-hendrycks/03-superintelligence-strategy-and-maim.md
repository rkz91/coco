## Central claim

A unilateral state push for superintelligence is impossible to hide and impossible to allow. The relative ease of sabotaging AI projects (cyberattacks on training infrastructure, targeting of key researchers, kinetic strikes on data centers as a worst case) makes any visible Manhattan-Project-style attempt to race to ASI strategically self-defeating. Therefore the strategic picture is already MAIM-like, whether the US articulates it or not.

## Verbatim MAIM definition

From the arXiv abstract:
> "Mutual Assured AI Malfunction (MAIM): a deterrence regime resembling nuclear mutual assured destruction (MAD) where any state's aggressive bid for unilateral AI dominance is met with preventive sabotage by rivals."

## Three-pillar framework

1. **Deterrence (MAIM).** Detect destabilizing AI projects; signal credible willingness to sabotage them. Cyber-espionage, technical surveillance of training runs, and as last resort kinetic action against data centers.
2. **Nonproliferation.** Track the location of AI chips; prevent terrorist groups and rogue states from acquiring weaponizable AI. Strong export controls, chip-level provenance, compute governance.
3. **Competitiveness.** Maintain domestic chip manufacturing and AI development capacity. Integrate AI into military planning, economic strategy, civilian infrastructure resilience. Note: this is competitive depth, not a winner-takes-all race.

## Why he opposes a "Manhattan Project for AI"

From the Economist op-ed (March 28, 2025):
- A trillion-dollar data center cannot be hidden.
- Concentrating "several hundred top researchers" in one location would be obvious to foreign intelligence.
- A visible race signals that the racer believes superintelligence is decisive — which is precisely the trigger for adversary sabotage.
- Therefore: stability comes from explicitly accepting deterrence-based equilibrium, not from trying to escape it.

## Critical reception (Zvi Mowshowitz)

Zvi's substack ("On MAIM and Superintelligence Strategy," https://thezvi.substack.com/p/on-maim-and-superintelligence-strategy) and RAND commentary (https://www.rand.org/pubs/commentary/2025/03/seeking-stability-in-the-competition-for-ai-advantage.html) generally treat the paper as the most serious attempt to import nuclear-strategy doctrine into AI policy, with disagreement primarily over (a) whether sabotage is in fact as feasible as Hendrycks claims, and (b) whether MAIM stabilizes or destabilizes if competitors disagree on what counts as "destabilizing."

## What this tells us about Hendrycks' policy worldview

- He treats AI development as inherently a national-security problem, not just a corporate-governance or alignment-research problem.
- He believes coalition-building between major-power states is the highest-leverage move, more important than internal lab safety work.
- He is comfortable explicitly importing Cold War deterrence doctrine and adapting it.
- He is willing to write joint papers with industry CEOs (Wang/Scale) and ex-tech-CEO-turned-defense-figures (Schmidt), signaling a coalition strategy that prioritizes influence over ideological purity.
- He does not believe a US-only safety regime is sufficient; without parallel restraint by China, restraint by the US is destabilizing rather than stabilizing.
