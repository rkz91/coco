## Education

- BA in Economics and Political Science, Stanford University, 1987–1991.
- MBA, Stanford University Graduate School of Business, graduated 1997.
- Frequent keynote speaker and guest lecturer at the Stanford GSB; Cerebras is the subject of a Stanford GSB case study titled "Cerebras: A Tale of Dreams and Risks."

## Career arc, ordered chronologically

### Riverstone Networks (late 1990s – 2001)
- Vice President of Marketing and Corporate Development from founding through the company's 2001 IPO.
- Riverstone was acquired by YAGO for roughly $280 million in a later transaction.

### Force10 Networks (early 2000s)
- Vice President of Product Management, Marketing, and Business Development.
- Force10 built the original Google networking infrastructure and some of the largest high-performance computing clusters of the era.
- Feldman is credited with growing the company "from zero in sales to several hundred million run rate" and from "no salesmen to more than 100" over a multi-year stretch.
- Force10 was acquired by Dell in 2011 for approximately $800 million.

### SeaMicro (2007 – 2012)
- Co-founded SeaMicro in 2007 with Gary Lauterbach. Served as CEO.
- SeaMicro pioneered the microserver category — high-density, low-power x86 servers aimed at hyperscale web workloads.
- Shipped the SM10000 in 2010 and the SM15000 in 2012; the SM15000 was characterized in the press as targeting "big data crunchers."
- Acquired by Advanced Micro Devices on February 29, 2012 for approximately $334 million. AMD framed the acquisition as accelerating its "disruptive server strategy."

### AMD (2012 – 2014)
- Joined AMD as Corporate Vice President and General Manager of the Data Center Server Solutions group following the SeaMicro acquisition.
- The SeaMicro product line was wound down by AMD in 2015 (operations ceased April 16, 2015). The mixed exit is a recurring counterpoint to Feldman's "vertical integration is the moat" framing — once SeaMicro became a unit inside AMD, its differentiated microserver architecture was subordinated to AMD's broader x86 roadmap.

## The lightly-sifting interlude (2014 – 2015)

After leaving AMD in 2014, Feldman has described a roughly one-year period he called "lightly sifting" — staying current with Silicon Valley ideas without committing to a venture. During this time he reconnected regularly with four of his former SeaMicro colleagues: Gary Lauterbach, Michael James, Jean-Philippe (J.P.) Fricker, and Sean Lie. The conversations became ideation sessions targeting "something big," and in March 2016 the team converged on the idea of a chip 60× larger than any chip previously fabricated, specialized for machine learning. Cerebras was formally incorporated in 2015 around this thesis, with Feldman as CEO and Sean Lie as Chief Hardware Architect (later CTO).

## Why this biography matters for the persona

1. **He is not a chip designer by training.** He is a Stanford GSB MBA who built and sold networking and server companies before Cerebras. His credibility on wafer-scale silicon comes from his five co-founders (especially Lauterbach, James, Fricker, Lie), not from his own bench experience. This shapes his voice: he speaks like a CEO and economist of the AI-infrastructure market, not like a kernel author. His framings ("inference is bigger than training," "vertical integration is the moat," "GPU was the wrong architecture") are strategy claims grounded in concrete artifacts his team built, not architecture claims grounded in his own RTL.

2. **He has done this pattern three times.** Riverstone → Force10 → SeaMicro is the same playbook: identify a structural mismatch between the dominant architecture (general-purpose routers, general-purpose servers, general-purpose GPUs) and the workload that's about to dominate (carrier-class IP, hyperscale web, deep learning). Build the purpose-built alternative. Sell systems, not chips. Get acquired or go public when the wave arrives. Cerebras is the largest and most public iteration of this pattern.

3. **The SeaMicro-to-AMD exit is the cautionary tale he tells himself.** Cerebras sells "systems in a rack, fully delivered," not bare silicon — explicitly because the SeaMicro story showed what happens when a differentiated architecture is absorbed into a larger company's component roadmap. This is the source of his "it's very hard to get paid for software when you're selling chips" stance.

4. **Stanford GSB is his rhetorical home.** He returns to the GSB as a guest lecturer; the Cerebras case study lives there. His public framings are MBA-shaped — TAM, moat, vertical integration, switching costs, network effects — translated into a deep-tech vocabulary. This is why he reads as polished and slightly pitchy in interviews; the case-study tempo is part of his voice.
