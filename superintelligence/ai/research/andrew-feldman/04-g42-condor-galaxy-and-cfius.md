## The G42 relationship

G42 is an Abu Dhabi technology holding group; the Cerebras–G42 relationship began with a July 2023 deal for multiple AI supercomputers. By 2024–2025, G42 was Cerebras's single largest customer:

- The G42 $335M minority investment accounted for approximately 83% of Cerebras's 2023 revenue.
- G42 represented 24% of 2025 revenues.
- The Condor Galaxy series of supercomputers (CG-1, CG-2, CG-3, and follow-ons) is the joint product of the partnership.

### Condor Galaxy progression

| System | Announced | Compute | Hardware |
|---|---|---|---|
| CG-1 | July 2023 | 4 exaFLOPs | 64 CS-2 systems |
| CG-2 | November 2023 | 4 exaFLOPs | 64 CS-2 systems |
| CG-3 | March 13, 2024 | 8 exaFLOPs | 64 CS-3 systems (Dallas, TX) |

CG-3 alone hosts 58 million AI-optimized cores, and the cumulative Condor Galaxy network reached 16 exaFLOPs of AI compute. Open models trained on Condor Galaxy include Jais-30B (Arabic), Med42 (medical), Crystal-Coder-7B, and BTLM-3B-8K.

## The CFIUS review and the September 2024 IPO withdrawal

The G42 minority investment triggered a Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS) review on national-security grounds. The concern: G42 had documented prior commercial ties to Huawei. With G42 accounting for the overwhelming majority of Cerebras's revenue and holding voting shares in a US AI-chip company, the review touched both supply-chain and capital-control concerns.

**Timeline:**
- September 30, 2024: Cerebras filed its S-1, targeting $750M–$1B at a $7B–$8B valuation.
- October 8, 2024: Reuters reported the CFIUS review delay was likely to postpone the IPO.
- Late 2024: Cerebras withdrew the filing. G42's voting shares were converted to non-voting shares, and Cerebras argued CFIUS lacked jurisdiction over a non-voting share sale.
- 2025: Company raised private capital instead, including the $1.1B Series G in September 2025 at an $8.1B valuation.
- January 2026: $1.0B Series H at $89.01/share (Benchmark $225M, Alpha Wave $100M, Fidelity $100M).
- May 14, 2026: IPO refiled and priced. Shares opened at $350 vs. an IPO price of $185, valuing Cerebras at roughly $95 billion at first-day close — the largest US tech IPO since Uber in 2019.

## Why this matters for the persona

The G42/CFIUS episode is the geopolitical seam under everything Feldman says publicly about supply chains, US chip manufacturing, and Asian capital. His May 14, 2026 Benzinga interview claim that catching up on US semiconductor manufacturing "could take 10 to 15 years" — and his explicit emphasis on TSMC as Cerebras's foundry partner since founding — both read differently when you understand that:

1. His company was nearly blocked from going public by a US national-security review.
2. His company's foundry dependency is in Taiwan, not the US.
3. His company's single largest customer base is in the Gulf, with all the export-controls fragility that implies.

When Feldman says **"this is a huge market, there's lots of opportunity… I think there'll be many winners,"** he is partly making a strategic claim about the inference TAM, and partly making a regulatory claim that AI compute should not be treated as zero-sum geopolitical contraband. The combination is deliberate.

The CFIUS episode is also why Feldman's IPO-cycle commentary leaned heavily on **"This was the right way to fund our growth"** (his post-IPO line to CNBC, May 14, 2026) — he had to publicly reframe a near-fatal regulatory delay as a deliberate financing choice. The conversion of voting shares to non-voting shares was the structural fix; the rhetorical fix was Feldman's job.

## The OpenAI deal (January 2026)

In January 2026, Cerebras signed a deal with OpenAI to deliver 750 megawatts of compute through 2028, valued at over $10 billion. The deal includes warrants for 33.4 million Cerebras shares contingent on OpenAI compute purchases. As part of the arrangement, Cerebras is "temporarily prohibited from selling its products to Anthropic" — a competitive carveout that signals how seriously OpenAI took the strategic value of dedicated Cerebras inference capacity.

On the No Priors podcast (post-IPO, 2026), Feldman described how the OpenAI deal "came together in only four weeks" — a velocity comment that is itself a strategic claim: Cerebras's customer-acquisition friction in inference is low because the switching cost from NVIDIA is "about 10 keystrokes."
