# Ilya Sutskever — Dwarkesh Patel interview (November 25, 2025)

Sources:
- https://www.dwarkesh.com/p/ilya-sutskever
- https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/iuKa2iPg7vD9BdZna/highlights-from-ilya-sutskever-s-november-2025-interview

Fetched: 2026-05-27

## Context

Sutskever's second-ever Dwarkesh interview. Released November 25, 2025. He is reclusive and gives few interviews, so this is a primary signal for his 2025-2026 worldview.

## On scaling vs. research (the three-eras framing, re-affirmed)

- "2012 to 2020 was an age of research. 2020 to 2025 was an age of scaling. 2026 onward will be another age of research."
- "Scaling the current thing will keep leading to improvements. In particular, it won't stall. But something important will continue to be missing."
- "Another 100x scaling of AI models would make a difference, but would not transform AI capabilities."

## On the limits of RL

- Companies now allocate more compute to RL than to self-supervised pre-training.
- RL consumes substantial compute but provides "a relatively small amount of learning" per FLOP.
- Implicit critique: the RL regime is the new bottleneck, and brute-forcing it is not the answer.

## On LLM generalization

- He expressed confusion about why LLMs perform well on benchmarks but fail practically.
- He characterized their generalization ability as "inadequate."
- Implication: benchmarks have been gamed; the cognitive gap is real and is the gating problem for superintelligence.

## On next-token prediction (carried forward from earlier views)

- "I challenge the claim that next-token prediction cannot surpass human performance."
- Sufficiently capable nets can extrapolate the behavior of hypothetical superior versions of humans not present in the training data.

## On the post-pre-training era

- "Already most of the default enforcement learning is coming from AIs. The humans are being used to train the reward function."
- Vision: humans do "1% of the work and the AI does 99%" in collaborative training loops.

## On data

- "The data situation is still quite good. There's still lots to go. But at some point the data will run out." (Slight softening from the NeurIPS 2024 "fossil fuel" framing.)

## On algorithmic improvements

- "There's only one way to find out." (Indicates uncertainty about magnitude of remaining algorithmic headroom.)

## On AGI timeline

- He resists specific predictions.
- Uses Tesla self-driving as an analogy: models appear capable but need work on "reliability, robustness, and well-behaved" performance.

## On safety / alignment

- "The more capable the model, the more confident we need to be."
- "I would not underestimate the difficulty of alignment of models that are actually smarter than us."

## On the inevitability of deep learning

- "Maybe like a year delayed?" — if key researchers had not existed. Sutskever's view is that deep learning advancement was largely inevitable given the hardware and data trajectory.

## On SSI specifically

- He was "cagey" about disclosing specific technical approaches.
- Stated his company is addressing the missing component but cannot share details publicly.
- This is a deliberate operating posture, not a refusal — SSI's strategy is to avoid showing its hand before the product is ready.

## Synthesis: Sutskever's late-2025 worldview

1. Pre-training is plateauing — not stopping, but producing diminishing returns.
2. RL has taken its place as the dominant compute sink, and RL is also limited.
3. The next breakthrough is in **generalization** — a missing component that is not parameter count, not data, and not RL flavor.
4. Safety scales with capability and becomes harder, not easier, as systems approach and surpass human intelligence.
5. SSI's bet is that this missing component can be found through focused research without commercial distraction.
