## Russell's Paris AI Safety Breakfast remarks (August 5, 2024)

The Future of Life Institute hosted a pre-Paris breakfast briefing on August 5, 2024. Russell was the inaugural speaker. Key positions:

### On Bletchley as the high-water mark

Russell credited UK organizers Ian Hogarth (then chair of UK AI Safety Institute) and Matt Clifford. Specifically praised:
- The Bletchley Declaration's explicit treatment of frontier AI as a catastrophic-risk category.
- The 28-country signatory list — meaningful breadth.
- The serious tone, with technical depth rather than "ethics-washing."

### On industry pushback

> "Industry has pushed back very hard." — Russell, FoLI Breakfast, August 2024

Specific cited examples:
- The attempt to weaken the EU AI Act by removing foundation-model clauses.
- Reframing of AI development around economic nationalism and great-power competition rather than safety.
- Coordinated industry lobbying to soften the Bletchley follow-through.

### On the substantive research priorities he wants summits to discuss

Russell consistently argues that the current LLM-scaling paradigm is the wrong target for safety attention. His preferred directions:

1. **Formal verification and mathematical guarantees** of system safety. "We need formal verification… we're just not doing those for AI systems."
2. **Neurosymbolic methods** that combine symbolic reasoning with learning, allowing inspection and proof.
3. **Probabilistic programming** as a more promising long-term substrate.
4. **Provably beneficial design** — the CIRL / off-switch-game agenda — rather than post-hoc evaluations.

### His critique of evals

> "Testing and evals are ineffective as a method of ensuring safety."

This is one of his more contrarian stances inside the safety community, where eval suites and red-teaming have become the dominant practical tool. Russell's argument is that evals can show the presence of unsafe behavior but cannot show the absence — and that for systems with arbitrary capability surfaces, the absence proof is what safety actually requires.

### His structural diagnosis

> "We've produced systems that many people regard as more intelligent than themselves. But we have no idea how they work."

The frame: capability has outrun comprehension. Until comprehension catches up — via interpretability, formal methods, or paradigm change — the responsible move is to slow capability rollout, not to lean harder on post-hoc evals.

### The headline ask

> "Stop, until you can figure out what happens if you succeed."

A direct echo of the 2023 FoLI pause letter (which Russell signed) but phrased as an ongoing posture, not a six-month moratorium.

## How Russell engages the summit track

- He is consistently **inside the tent** — speaks at official side events, briefs national delegations, advises on framing — rather than outside protesting.
- He uses **comparison to other governance regimes** (IAEA nuclear, IPCC climate, ICAO aviation, chemical weapons convention) as the move that converts AI safety from a Silicon Valley concern into a familiar diplomatic problem.
- He is unusually willing to **name industry lobbying as the cause** of summit drift, which differentiates him from more diplomatically careful AI-safety voices.

## Significance for the persona

The summit track is the public record of Russell's policy stances over a rolling 30-month window. Recent signals for this persona should always check the latest summit (next is reportedly India in 2027) and his commentary track around it.
