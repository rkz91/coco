## 3. Canonical concepts and works (verified, with dates)

- **Aggregation Theory** — foundational article published **July 21, 2015** (`https://stratechery.com/2015/aggregation-theory/`; concept hub `https://stratechery.com/concept/aggregation-theory/`). Core thesis: in the pre-internet world businesses won by controlling **supply** (printing presses, broadcast licenses, shelf space); on the internet, **distribution and transaction costs fall to zero**, so the durable advantage shifts to **owning the demand side / the user relationship**. Aggregators (Google, Facebook/Meta, Amazon, Netflix, Uber, Airbnb) win by owning users, which forces suppliers to commoditize and modularize to reach those users. Builds on Christensen's **Conservation of Attractive Profits**.
- **The Smiling Curve** — `https://stratechery.com/concept/aggregation-theory/smiling-curve/`; "Publishers and the Smiling Curve" (2014, `https://stratechery.com/2014/publishers-smiling-curve/`). Value flows to the two ends of the value chain — **aggregators** (own demand) and **differentiated suppliers/brands** — and is crushed out of the **middle** (the old point of integration, e.g. newspapers, distributors). Internet destroyed the value of integration in the middle.
- **"What Clayton Christensen Got Wrong"** — **September 22, 2013** (`https://stratechery.com/2013/clayton-christensen-got-wrong/`). THE source for the productive conflict with Christensen. Core critique:
  - Disruption theory's low-end-disruption mechanism relies on **B2B rational spec-based buyers** (disk drives, steel, medical devices). It does **not** predict consumer markets, where purchasing is driven by **un-measurable** attributes — ease-of-use, user experience.
  - Key quote: **"It is impossible for a user experience to be too good."** Therefore consumer experience cannot be "overshot," which exempts Apple from the disruption trap.
  - Quote: **"every attribute that matters can not be documented and measured"** (in consumer markets).
  - Advocates Michael Porter's generic-strategy framework (differentiation as durable advantage) over disruption for consumer products.
- **"AI Integration and Modularization"** (2024, `https://stratechery.com/2024/ai-integration-and-modularization/`) — applies the integration-vs-modularity lens to the AI stack.
- **"Disruption is not a strategy"** framing — recurring Thompson refrain that disruption theory is descriptive/predictive, not a prescriptive *strategy* a company can adopt; pairs with his Christensen critique.

## 4. Recent signals (12-month window, all post-2025-06-01) — VERIFIED DATES

1. **"Aggregators and AI"** — published **Friday, May 30, 2026** (`https://stratechery.com/2026/aggregators-and-ai/`). Argument: AI is a **sustaining** technology for network-effects aggregators, not a disruptive one. Spotify is the exemplar — every user already gets an individualized version of the product, so AI personalization deepens the existing moat rather than undermining it. "Monolithic network-effects companies are poised to be big winners from AI."
2. **"The Chip Fly in the AI Ointment"** — **Friday, May 30, 2026** (`https://stratechery.com/2026/the-chip-fly-in-the-ai-ointment/`). Argument: chips matter more than ever; structural TSMC dependence means that "if demand doesn't take the initiative to restructure supply [then] the shortages looming by the end of the decade could dramatically curtail AI's impact." (Draws on his semiconductor/Taiwan expertise.)
3. **"Agents Over Bubbles"** — **Monday, March 16, 2026** (`https://stratechery.com/2026/agents-over-bubbles/`). Argument: he no longer thinks AI is a bubble. Three LLM paradigms — ChatGPT (training-heavy), o1-style reasoning (inference-heavy), and **agents** (model + harness that self-verifies). Agents are the watershed: "By abstracting humans away from direct model management any one single human can control multiple agents… it actually won't require that many people with agency to drastically increase the amount of compute that is actively utilized." Integration between model and harness makes Anthropic/OpenAI defensibly profitable, contra commoditization arguments.
4. **"Microsoft and Software Survival"** — **Tuesday, February 3, 2026** (`https://stratechery.com/2026/microsoft-and-software-survival/`). Argument: software/SaaS is NOT dead. "writing an app is a commitment to a never-ending journey… that has nothing to do with the company's core competency"; "software companies are not losers, but rather winners: they will be able to write more code more efficiently." Microsoft survives via enterprise identity (Active Directory), Work IQ agents, M365, GitHub Copilot.
5. **Cheeky Pint interview (Stripe / John Collison)** — **February 12, 2026** (`https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/ben-thompson-from-stratechery-on`). On AI ads, end of SaaS, future of media. Quotes: "I think ads are amazing"; "Meta ads are more broadly valuable because they're showing me stuff I didn't know that I wanted"; "The internet enables the creation of a million different ponds… be the only biggest fish in that pond." Prefers Meta-style broad user-profiling ads over context/search-bound ads; calls OpenAI's banner ads "the bare minimum easiest solution." On SaaS: shift from growth to stability; seat-based pricing under pressure as hiring slows; systems-of-record (Workday, Stripe Billing) stay defensible.
6. **"AI Power, Now and In 100 Years"** & **"AI and the Human Condition"** — 2026 (`https://stratechery.com/2026/ai-power-now-and-in-100-years/`, `https://stratechery.com/2026/ai-and-the-human-condition/`). Recurring 2026 theme: technology is **amoral**; what matters is the decisions humans make about it; humans will keep wanting other humans, and economies will reflect that.

## 6. Mental models

- Aggregation Theory (own demand, not supply).
- Smiling Curve (ends win, middle loses).
- Integration vs. modularity / Conservation of Attractive Profits (Christensen-derived; profits sit at the scarce, integrated layer).
- Zero distribution + zero transaction cost as the internet's prime mover.
- "Strategy credit" / strategy vs. tactics — judging companies on durable structural advantage, not single moves.
- Technology is amoral; outcomes are a function of human choices and incentives.
