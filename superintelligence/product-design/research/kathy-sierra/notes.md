- **2003–2005:** Co-created the **Head First** book series with **Bert Bates**, beginning with *Head First Java* (2003). "Unorthodox, visually intensive approach" to teaching programming. Three Jolt Award nominations; **won 2005 for *Head First Design Patterns***. Amazon top-10 computer books list 2003–2005. Source: Wikipedia.
- **Creating Passionate Users blog:** Prominent early-2000s blog on "neuroscience, presentations, and how to build software that makes users kick ass." Among the most-read tech blogs of its era. Source: Wikipedia; multiple secondary.

Robert Scoble and others briefly suspended their blogs in solidarity. Source: Wikipedia (citing BBC News 2007, NYT Magazine 2008, Wired).
- **July 2013:** Launched **Serious Pony** (seriouspony.com) — blog + Twitter — "mostly about the science of badass, with a little UX, learning theory, game design, DSLR video, horses, and code." Source: Wikipedia; seriouspony.com/about.

## 2. Core thesis — "Make the USER awesome, not the product"

- Central thesis: successful products create **expert users**. To create an expert user, the team must design a path that helps the customer **gain mastery in a domain related to the product**. Source: mtlynch.io book report; sebastienphlix summary.
- "Many people don't care how awesome your product is. They care about how awesome **they** are when they use your product. We make our users badass when our product helps them achieve badass results."
- Direct quote (recurring across talks): **"People don't use your app because they like the app or they like you, they are using it because they like themselves, and they tell their friends because they like their friends!"** Source: Building Winning Products / Medium (Paul Pedrazzi); BoS 2012.
- A product should be judged not on how it works or how it looks, but on **what it enables the user to accomplish** and **how it makes the user feel**.
- **Word of mouth is a side-effect of making users awesome**, not of brand loyalty: "they tell their friends not because they like you, but because they like their friends." Source: BoS 2012.
- "Badass" = an **expert** — somebody who has learned a skill, crossed the **"valley of suck,"** and achieved **mastery** / "reliably superior performance." Source: mtlynch.io; Building Winning Products.

## 3. The Badass framework (five components)

From the "Designing for Badass" / *Badass* analysis (Building Winning Products, Medium; BoS 2012 "Building the Minimum Badass User"):

1. **Define Badass** — templates linking specific situations to expert-level performance (e.g., "given harsh portrait lighting, make appropriate aesthetic exposures").
2. **Model Excellence** — repeated exposure to high-quality examples. **"Practice makes permanent"** — the brain absorbs what it repeatedly experiences, so avoid exposing users to mediocrity.
3. **Deliberate Practice** — structure skill-building to move users from unreliable to **80–90% reliability within 1–3 sessions** via fine-grained challenges and **low-latency feedback**.
4. **Forward Flow** — a clear, believable **progression path** from novice to expert; users always know where they stand and what comes next.
5. **Manage Cognitive Load** — protect limited willpower by reducing choices, confusion, and frustration through simplicity and smart defaults.

- **Superpowers / compelling context:** position users in a larger context — shift the goal from "master this tool" to "achieve this outcome." "Read a cookbook" → "become a great chef"; "master Final Cut Pro" → "make killer videos." Design from the user's **endgame**, not from mastery of your offering. Source: Building Winning Products.
- **Expertise = "reliably superior performance"** — consistency, not merely more knowledge or experience. Source: Building Winning Products.
- **"Minimum Badass User"** — design for users who reach genuine expertise, not casual users. Such users become **fault-tolerant**: they tolerate product imperfections because the tool genuinely enables meaningful achievement. "It's not about quality, it's about user awesome." Source: BoS 2012 talk.

## 4. Cognitive-resources / anti-engagement argument ("Your App Makes Me Fat")

- Original post: **"Your App Makes Me Fat,"** Serious Pony, **July 24, 2013**. Source: businessofsoftware.org/2023/09/...
- References **Baba Shiv's 1999 research**: cognitive exertion depletes the **same pool** as self-control. Direct quote: **"Willpower and cognitive processing draw from the _same_ pool of resources."**
- A **cognitive leak** is anything that takes brain cycles. These resources are rare and precious; smart design spots cognitive leaks and stops them. Examples: meetings; small interface changes; counter-intuitive behavior. **"If an app behaves counter-intuitively even just once, users will leak cognitive resources every time they use it, forever, wondering whether the action did what they expected."** Source: lireo.com (Fluent Conference write-up); interaction-design.org.
- Implication / quote: **"Your app might be the one place your user _wants_ to spend those resources. But knowing that interacting with our product comes at a precious cost, maybe we'll make different choices."**
- This is the **direct anti-engagement / anti-gamification stance**: persuasive design and gamification deplete users' resources for the company's benefit, not the user's. This is the cleanest cited basis for the **productive conflict with Nir Eyal** (user-awesome vs product-engagement) — Sierra's whole frame argues against optimizing for time-in-app and habit hooks.
