## Disruptive innovation — core theory (verified)

- **Definition (HBR 2015, "What Is Disruptive Innovation?", Christensen / Raynor / McDonald):** Disruption is **a process, not a product or a fixed point in time**, by which a smaller company with fewer resources successfully challenges established incumbents. Entrants take root at the **low end** (over-served customers who'll accept a "good enough," cheaper offering) or in a **new-market foothold** (non-consumers the incumbent doesn't serve at all), then **move upmarket** while incumbents — rationally chasing higher-margin customers — **flee upmarket** and cede the low end, until the disruptor's improved product satisfies the mainstream and topples them.
  - URL: https://hbr.org/2015/12/what-is-disruptive-innovation
- **The Innovator's Dilemma:** incumbents fail *because* they do everything right — they listen to their best customers and invest in sustaining innovations with the highest margins. Good management is precisely what makes them vulnerable to disruption. Examples in the book: mainframes disrupted by minicomputers then PCs; integrated steel mills disrupted by minimills; disk-drive industry generational transitions.
  - URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Innovator%27s_Dilemma
- **Sustaining vs. disruptive:** sustaining innovation improves products for existing demanding customers along established performance dimensions; disruptive innovation initially *underperforms* on those dimensions but wins on new ones (price, accessibility, simplicity, convenience).
- **Uber is NOT disruptive (Christensen's own correction, HBR 2015):** Uber started with mainstream customers (not a low-end or new-market foothold) and its quality was not initially inferior. By his strict definition it is a *sustaining* innovation in the taxi market, not a disruptive one. He used this to argue the term had been catastrophically misused — "disruptive" had come to mean "any clever startup that shakes up incumbents," which guts the theory's predictive power.
  - URL: https://hbr.org/2015/12/what-is-disruptive-innovation
- **"Disruption 2020" — MIT Sloan Management Review interview (one of his last):** revisits and defends the theory near the end of his life.
  - URL: https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/an-interview-with-clayton-m-christensen/
- **HBR "The Essential Clayton Christensen Articles" (Jan 2020, posthumous collection):** HBR's curated canon of his most important pieces.
  - URL: https://hbr.org/2020/01/the-essential-clayton-christensen-articles

## Jobs-to-be-Done — origin and the milkshake study (verified)

- **The lineage / three-camp tension (important for `pairs_well_with` and `productive_conflict_with`):**
  - **Tony Ulwick** conceptualized the outcome-driven approach in 1990 (Six Sigma applied to innovation), named it **Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)** in 1999, and introduced it to Christensen. Ulwick's camp is the *rigorous, quantitative* JTBD camp ("jobs + desired outcomes as metrics").
  - **Christensen** coined the phrase **"Jobs to Be Done"** (~2003, *The Innovator's Solution*; popularized in the 2005 "Marketing Malpractice" HBR article with Scott Cook and Taddy Hall) and gave it the famous narrative framing.
  - **Bob Moesta** worked alongside Christensen and developed the **"switch" / demand-side timeline interview** practice — the qualitative, story-based JTBD camp. Moesta is credited as a co-creator of the *practical* JTBD method and the person who actually ran much of the milkshake fieldwork.
  - These three camps (Ulwick's ODI / Moesta's switch interviews / Christensen's theory) are a genuine, ongoing tension in JTBD practice — the ROSTER explicitly wires the "Torres↔Ulwick↔Moesta three-camp JTBD tension."
  - URLs: https://strategyn.com/jobs-to-be-done/ ; https://therewiredgroup.com/case-studies/milkshakes/ ; https://gopractice.io/product/jobs-to-be-done-the-theory-and-the-frameworks/
- **The milkshake study:** McDonald's wanted to sell more milkshakes. Christensen's team (with Moesta) observed that ~40% of milkshakes were bought early in the morning, by solo commuters, to go. The "job" was: make a long, boring commute more interesting and stave off mid-morning hunger, in a form you can manage one-handed in a car. The milkshake competed not with other milkshakes but with **bagels, doughnuts, bananas, Snickers bars, and coffee**. Lesson: segment by *job*, not by *customer demographic* or *product category*.
  - URL: https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/clay-christensens-milkshake-marketing
- **The "hire / fire" framing:** "People don't buy products; they hire them to make progress in their lives." If a product does the job, you hire it again; if not, you fire it. *Competing Against Luck* (2016) is the canonical book.
  - URL: https://www.fullstory.com/blog/clayton-christensen-jobs-to-be-done-framework-product-development/
- **The Theodore Levitt drill quote:** Christensen popularized Levitt's "People don't want a quarter-inch drill; they want a quarter-inch hole" in the 2005 "Marketing Malpractice" article as the seed of jobs thinking. (Quote-origin trail actually runs through Leo McGivena 1942/1947; Levitt popularized it; Christensen attributed it to Levitt.)
  - URLs: https://quoteinvestigator.com/2019/03/23/drill/ ; https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16334583/ (Marketing Malpractice, PubMed record)

## The disruption critiques (for `productive_conflict_with`)

### Ben Thompson (`ben-thompson` on the ROSTER) — "disruption is not a strategy" / consumer-market critique
- Thompson is a self-described "huge fan" who says disruption theory is "**95% right**" — but the **5% matters**. He has critiqued **low-end disruption** since ~2010 (as an MBA student) and built it into Stratechery.
- Core critique: Christensen's theory was built on **B2B buying decisions** where buyers evaluate a feature checklist and will accept "good enough." It **fails in consumer markets**, where individuals buy on *experience*, not a checklist — and people want a *better* experience "infinitely." So the most important modern companies (Google, Facebook, Amazon, Netflix, Uber, Airbnb) **won from the top down** with superior UX, not from the bottom up with "good enough."
- Thompson's alternative, **Aggregation Theory**, explains internet-era winners better than low-end disruption does.
- Notably: Thompson **agrees with Christensen** that Uber is not disruptive — but for different reasons, and uses it to show the theory's domain is narrower than its fame.
- URLs:
  - https://stratechery.com/2014/critiquing-disruption-theory/
  - https://stratechery.com/aggregation-theory/
  - https://stratechery.com/2015/beyond-disruption/
- This is the **real, citable conflict** the brief asks for: Thompson vs. Christensen on "disruption is not a strategy / the theory over-reaches in consumer markets."

### Jill Lepore — "The Disruption Machine" (The New Yorker, June 23, 2014)
- Harvard historian Jill Lepore attacked the empirical basis of disruption theory: re-read Christensen's case studies and found alternative readings; called his sources "often dubious and his logic questionable"; argued disruption advocates make circular arguments.
- Christensen's response (BusinessWeek/Bloomberg interview): called it "a criminal act of dishonesty — at Harvard, of all places," and defended the theory while conceding the *term* had become a cliché.
- URLs: https://longreads.com/2014/06/18/the-disruption-machine/ ; https://slate.com/technology/2014/06/clayton-christensen-jill-lepore-agree-disruptive-innovation-has-become-a-cliche.html
