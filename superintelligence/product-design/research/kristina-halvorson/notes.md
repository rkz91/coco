## The canonical definition (load-bearing — appears verbatim across sources)

> "Content strategy plans for the creation, publication, and governance of useful, usable content."
— Kristina Halvorson, "The Discipline of Content Strategy," *A List Apart*, **December 16, 2008**.
https://alistapart.com/article/thedisciplineofcontentstrategy/

Updated form in the Content Strategy Quad era:
> Content strategy is **"guiding the creation, delivery, and governance of useful, usable content."** As a practice it helps teams **"define, prioritize, integrate, systematize, and measure content."**
— Brain Traffic, "New Thinking: Brain Traffic's Content Strategy Quad."
https://www.braintraffic.com/blog/new-thinking-brain-traffics-content-strategy-quad

### From "The Discipline of Content Strategy" (A List Apart, 2008-12-16)
- Core thesis: web professionals obsessed over UX, IA, and visual design while treating **content as "somebody else's problem."** Content deserves recognition as a **distinct, critical discipline** requiring dedicated expertise and resources.
- Strategy must address **WHY** content is published, not just what — otherwise it is "a glorified production line for content nobody really needs or wants."
- Distinguishes **web writing** (producing content for online publication; requires UX/IA understanding) from **editorial strategy** (governance: voice, tone, content lifecycles).
- Component model — six interrelated practices: editorial strategy, web writing, metadata strategy, SEO, content management strategy, content channel distribution strategy.
- Epigraph she leans on: "Discipline is remembering what you want." (attributed to David Campbell, Saks Fifth Avenue founder.)

## The Content Strategy Quad (Brain Traffic's evolved model)

Source: https://www.braintraffic.com/blog/new-thinking-brain-traffics-content-strategy-quad (model framing originated ~2014; "Quad" article references 2018)

Two top-level halves, four quadrants:
1. **Content Design** = Editorial strategy (mission, audience, voice, tone, brand standards) + Experience design (user needs, content ecosystem, customer journeys, formats, design patterns).
2. **Systems Design** = Content structure/engineering (organization, tagging, categorization, reuse, personalization) + Process design (lifecycle management, tools, accountability, standards, metrics).
- The quad is deliberately rotated 90° so **systems design sits at the bottom** — "content systems provide the foundation for successful content design."
- Adopts **Sarah Richards'** content-design definition: "the process of using data and evidence to give the audience the content they need, at the time they need it, and in a way they expect."
