## The 5-component positioning framework (Obviously Awesome)

Sources:
- <https://www.heinzmarketing.com/blog/five-components-of-effective-positioning-an-obviously-awesome-book-summary-part-2/>
- <https://www.kathirvel.com/guide-april-dunford-positioning-framework/>
- <https://saasclub.io/podcast/5-steps-saas-product-positioning-with-april-dunford-252/>

The five components, worked **in this order**:
1. **Competitive alternatives** — what your best-fit customers would actually use instead of you (often "do nothing" or a spreadsheet, not a named rival).
2. **Unique attributes** — capabilities/features you have that the alternatives do not.
3. **Value (and proof)** — the benefit those attributes enable that customers care about; the stepping stone from features to "why it matters."
4. **Target market characteristics** — the customers who care *most* about that value; segment defined by value, not demographics.
5. **Market category** — the context you put the product in so the value is obvious to that segment.
6. (Bonus) **Relevant trends** — layered on top, carefully, to make the product feel current without chasing irrelevant hype.

Key relational insight (Heinz summary, quoting the book): value is dependent on unique attributes, which are only "unique" relative to a competitive alternative; the target segment is the people who care most about that value; the category is "the context I can wrap around my product that makes this value obvious to these people." You must flow through the components **in order** to land differentiated positioning, and always from input from best-fit customers, never assumptions.

## Sales Pitch (2023) — the narrative structure

Sources:
- <https://www.airtree.vc/open-source-vc/how-to-build-a-sales-pitch-that-wins>
- <https://aprildunford.substack.com/p/sales-first-storytelling> (publ. 2023-11-29)
- <https://pagetwo.com/book/sales-pitch/>

*Sales Pitch* (Ambient Press / Page Two, published **2023-08-28**) translates positioning into a sales narrative. The pitch structure is roughly:

1. **Insight / setup** — the rep's point of view on the market.
2. **Alternatives** — an honest survey of how else the problem gets solved (the "you could do nothing / use a spreadsheet / use a competitor" map).
3. **Perfect world** — what the ideal solution would look like (sets the criteria).
4. **Introduction** — finally introduce your product.
5. **Differentiated value** (often shown via demo).
6. **Proof**.
7. **Objections**.
8. (the book frames it as an **eight-step** structure; some summaries collapse to seven; "the ask" closes it.)

Core arguments captured:
- Research finding she cites: B2B software buyers most want (1) the seller's **perspective on the whole market**, and (2) **help navigating alternatives** — not a product-feature monologue.
- "Sales and marketing are DIFFERENT." "Our primary job in sales is to help guide prospects through the purchase process." "Modifying a sales pitch to work for marketing is easier than the reverse" — build from the sales narrative outward. (substack, 2023-11-29)
- Lead with **market insight**, not a generic problem statement a competitor could also claim.

## Category creation stance

Sources:
- <https://www.panblastpr.com/resources/category-creation-positioning-april-dunford/>
- <https://aprildunford.substack.com/p/understanding-the-job-of-a-market>

> "I don't believe that companies create categories. I believe that categories emerge, and some companies are wise to that."

Argument: category creation is the hardest positioning style — you must convince buyers both that the **problem deserves a category** and that **your solution wins** — roughly double the work for the same conversion. Generally only big, venture-backed companies with patient investors can afford it. Her own podcast episode title: "Category Creation is Something You Don't Want to Do Unless You Have To." Finding/joining an *emerging* category beats trying to manufacture one.

## Positioning is NOT branding, NOT marketing's job

Source: <https://marketinghistory.org/p/april-dunford-positioning> (publ. **2026-04-02**)

> "positioning should inform what the branding looks like. But ... I still see those two things as being distinct."

> "it's definitely not just marketing, right?"

> "Marketing never wins the battle of opinions."

Argument: branding is tone/iconography/fonts/colors/imagery — the "vibe." Positioning is *why customers should choose you over competitors*. Positioning is cross-functional: the **CEO sponsors** it; **sales** best identifies the real competitive alternatives; **product** knows what differentiates; **marketing** owns the value framing; all inform segmentation. Without cross-functional alignment, positioning fails ("marketing never wins the battle of opinions").

## AI-era positioning

Sources:
- <https://blog.logrocket.com/product-management-launchpod-april-dunfords-1-killer-question-expose-weak-ai-product-positioning/> (LaunchPod episode, publ. **2026-01-16**)
- <https://www.theremoteworktribe.com/2025/10/02/the-remote-work-tribe-podcast-april-dunford/> (publ. **2025-10-02**)

Arguments:
- "We have AI" is **not** positioning. The "AI washing" trap: saying you have AI is not a strategy.
- Anchor positioning in **outcomes competitors can't match**, not in the presence of AI capability.
- Use competitors who **over-promise AI features** strategically — their exaggeration becomes your differentiation opportunity.
- Weak positioning has concrete costs: **wasted roadmaps, misleading sales feedback, engineering burnout** on features that don't win.
- The Remote Work Tribe (2025-10-02): discusses mindset shifts since the book, category creation, internal alignment/friction, and positioning in the AI era.
