## Biographical facts (verified)

- **Born:** February 5, 1954, Ithaca, New York. Jewish-American family. Father was a University of Pennsylvania professor and director of the Monell Chemical Senses Center; her mother taught counted-thread embroidery (a craft Kare repeatedly cites as the ancestor of pixel art). Brother: aerospace engineer Jordin Kare (d. 2017).
- **Education:**
  - Harriton High School (graduated 1971). Andy Hertzfeld was a high-school friend from age 14 — the connection that later brought her to Apple.
  - B.A. in Art, *summa cum laude*, Mount Holyoke College, 1975.
  - M.A. and Ph.D. in Fine Arts, New York University, 1978. Dissertation on caricature in the sculpture of Honoré Daumier and Claes Oldenburg.
- **Pre-Apple:** Moved to San Francisco; worked at the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco as a sculptor and occasional curator. In 1982 she was welding a life-size razorback-hog sculpture (an Arkansas museum commission) when Hertzfeld called.
- **Personal:** Formerly married to Jay Tannenbaum (dissolved 2011); three sons. Runs a digital-design practice / studio in San Francisco. Sells limited-edition signed fine-art prints at kareprints.com (since ~2011).

### Career timeline (verified)
- **Apple Computer, 1982–1986.** Officially hired January 1983, badge #3978, title "Macintosh Artist" (later "HI [Human Interface] Designer / Bitmap Artist"). Designed the original Mac's visual language in roughly a year ahead of the January 24, 1984 launch.
- **NeXT, 1986.** Employee #10 and Creative Director for Steve Jobs's second company. She hired/introduced Paul Rand to create the NeXT cube logo. Left because she "wanted to be back doing bitmaps."
- **Independent practice, 1986/1988/1989 onward.** Founded her design firm (variously cited as Susan Kare Design 1988 / Susan Kare LLP 1989). Client base: Microsoft, IBM, Sony Pictures, Motorola, General Magic, Intel, Autodesk, Eazel, Chumby, Facebook, Pinterest, Ledger.
- **U.S. Citizens Coinage Advisory Committee, appointed 2003** (by Speaker Nancy Pelosi).
- **Facebook, 2006–2010.** Designed hundreds of 64×64-pixel "virtual gift" icons.
- **Pinterest, 2015.** Hired as a product design lead — her first in-house corporate role in roughly three decades.
- **Niantic Labs, since February 2021.** Design Architect. Current primary affiliation; she also continues to run her own studio.

### Canonical works (verified)
- **Bitmap typefaces:** Chicago (the system font; bold, proportionally spaced — the first proportionally spaced digital font family; originally codenamed/named "Elefont"), Geneva (proportional sans), Monaco (monospace), New York, and the icon font Cairo (the "proto-emoji" set, source of the Dogcow glyph "z").
- **Icons / interface metaphors:** Happy Mac and Sad Mac (boot/error), the Trash can, the dog-eared document, the I-beam text cursor, the watch/wristwatch "wait" cursor, the Lasso, the Grabber (hand), the Paint Bucket, scissors (cut), the paintbrush, the pointing finger, and the system-error "bomb." Clarus the Dogcow (the page-setup mascot, "Moof!").
- **The ⌘ Command-key symbol.** When Steve Jobs decided that using the Apple logo throughout the menus was "over-using" the logo, Kare — days from deadline — found the **looped square** (a "Bowen knot" / "Saint John's Arms" / "Saint Hannes cross") in an international symbol dictionary, where it marks "place of interest" / cultural sites on Nordic (esp. Swedish) campground and roadside maps. She rendered it as a 16×16 bitmap. It is now encoded at Unicode U+2318. Deep historical roots: looped-square forms appear on ancient Gotland picture-stones.
- **Microsoft:** the Windows 3.0 **Solitaire** card deck (1990) — explicitly designed to teach new users mouse drag-and-drop; plus Windows 3.0 wallpaper and isometric icons (many unchanged through Windows XP).
- **IBM OS/2** pinstriped isometric icons; **General Magic** Magic Cap's cartoon-office desktop; **Eazel/Nautilus** file-manager icons (donated to the public).

### Awards (verified, dated)
- **2001 (October):** Chrysler Design Award.
- **2015:** MoMA acquired her original 1982–83 grid sketchbook / GUI icon sketches for the permanent collection.
- **April 2018:** AIGA Medal (the field's highest honor). AIGA profile by Zachary Crockett, Sept 17, 2018.
- **October 2019:** Cooper Hewitt National Design Award for Lifetime Achievement.
- **2025:** Featured in MoMA's *Pirouette: Turning Points in Design* (see recency signals).

## Design philosophy & process (verified, for public_stances and voice)

- **Icons as traffic signs.** "Good icons should function somewhat like traffic signs — simple symbols with few extraneous details, which makes them more universal." (AIGA / widely quoted.)
- **The one-time-tell test of memorability.** "An icon is successful if you could tell someone what it is once and they don't forget it." Her stated triad: **meaning, memorability, clarity.**
- **Universality / inclusion.** "Make things that are simple so you don't exclude anybody" / "always make it universal."
- **Paul Rand's maxim, which she adopted:** "Don't try to be original, just try to be good."
- **Constraints as a gift.** On the 32×32 grid: "there were 32 pixels in about half an inch. So an icon was 32×32, around a thousand pixels — quite a bit of freedom, even in a small space with one color." The constraint forces every pixel to justify its existence. She started icons on a $2.50 grid notebook of the smallest graph paper from the University Art store in Palo Alto (Hertzfeld's suggestion) because she had no computer yet — mocking up commands ("boot," "debug") pixel-by-pixel, translating technical jargon into visual metaphor.
- **Craft lineage / "nothing new under the sun."** She repeatedly grounds pixel art in pre-digital craft — mosaics, needlepoint, counted-thread embroidery (her mother's craft), and pointillism. From the 2026 Ledger feature: "You study art history and realize there's nothing new under the sun." And: "have you ever seen some 18th-century needlepoint (art)?"
- **Durability over novelty.** "Nobody seems to need to redesign the stop sign every two years." (Ledger Podcast, Nov 2025.)
- **Personality over literalism / collaboration.** On the Nano Gen5 badges she designed for "personalities" rather than literal objects (analogizing to how twelve zodiac signs stand in for billions of people), and: "I like to surround myself with people who are open to jumping on these things"; "I usually think you end up with something better when there are more ideas."
- **Humanizing the machine.** The throughline of her career is giving the computer "a face" — warmth, friendliness, and humor (the smiling Mac, the Dogcow) so that a hostile, command-line machine became approachable. This is the "design of desire" framing she and Tony Fadell used at Ledger Op3n 2025.
