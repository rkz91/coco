## 1. Biography and career timeline

- **Born** 20 May 1932 in Wiesbaden, Hesse-Nassau, Germany. Grandfather was a carpenter — early formative influence on craft and material honesty. (Wikipedia)
- Studied **architecture and interior decoration at the Wiesbaden School of Art (Werkkunstschule Wiesbaden), 1947–1953**, with an interruption for a carpentry apprenticeship. (Wikipedia)
- **Joined Braun in 1955** at age 23, originally as an architect / interior designer recruited to modernise the company's interiors. Mentored at Braun by **Fritz Eichler, Hans Gugelot, and Otl Aicher** (the latter two associated with the Ulm School of Design / Hochschule für Gestaltung Ulm, the post-Bauhaus functionalist school). (Wikipedia)
- **Head of design at Braun from 1961**, serving ~34 years until **retirement in 1997** at age 65. Over his Braun tenure he produced or oversaw **more than 500 products**. (Wikipedia; Vitsœ)
- **Vitsœ collaboration began 1957–1959.** In 1957 Rams asked Erwin Braun if he could design furniture for another company; Braun's answer: "Yes. It will help the market for our radios." Vitsœ (originally Vitsoe+Zapf) was founded in 1959; the **606 Universal Shelving System launched in 1960** and is still manufactured essentially unchanged ~66 years later. (Vitsœ "Good design"; Vitsœ "Dieter Rams")
- Rams's own framing: *"In 1957 I began to develop a storage system that formed the basis of the company Vitsœ, which was founded in 1959. Thus, the ideology behind my design is engrained within the company."* (Vitsœ)
- Established the **Dieter and Ingeborg Rams Foundation** using his 1992 IKEA prize funds. Wife **Ingeborg Rams** was a photographer and collaborator. (Wikipedia; CAFA)
- **Status in 2026:** living, age 94; long retired from active design practice. Treated as **archetype** — profile is drawn from the canonical published corpus and enduring principles, not from current design output. (Wikipedia)

## 2. Canonical product designs (with years)

- **SK 4 Phonosuper** (1956, with Hans Gugelot) — radiogram with a clear acrylic lid, nicknamed "Snow White's coffin" (Schneewittchensarg). (Wikipedia)
- **T3 pocket transistor radio** (1958) — widely cited as the visual ancestor of the original iPod's click-wheel face. (Wikipedia)
- **TP1 portable radio/record player** (1959). (Wikipedia)
- **Vitsœ 606 Universal Shelving System** (1960) — the longest-lived; still sold. (Vitsœ)
- **Vitsœ 620 Chair Programme** (1962). (Wikipedia)
- **T2 cylindric lighter** (1968). (Wikipedia)
- **Regie 510 receiver** (1972). (Wikipedia)
- **ET 66 calculator** (1987, with Dietrich Lubs) — the visual reference for Apple's iOS Calculator app. (Wikipedia)
- Braun clocks and watches (with Dietrich Lubs) — referenced by the iOS clock app. (Wikipedia; CAFA)

## 3. The Ten Principles of Good Design (verbatim, Vitsœ source of truth)

Framing: in the late 1970s, troubled by "an impenetrable confusion of forms, colours and noises," Rams asked himself **"Is my design good design?"** The answer became the ten principles. (Vitsœ "Good design")

1. **Good design is innovative.** "The possibilities for innovation are not, by any means, exhausted. Technological development is always offering new opportunities for innovative design. But innovative design always develops in tandem with innovative technology, and can never be an end in itself."
2. **Good design makes a product useful.** "A product is bought to be used... Good design emphasizes the usefulness of a product whilst disregarding anything that could possibly detract from it."
3. **Good design is aesthetic.** "The aesthetic quality of a product is integral to its usefulness because products we use every day affect our person and our well-being. But only well-executed objects can be beautiful."
4. **Good design makes a product understandable.** "It clarifies the product's structure. Better still, it can make the product talk. At best, it is self-explanatory."
5. **Good design is unobtrusive.** "Products fulfilling a purpose are like tools. They are neither decorative objects nor works of art... neutral and restrained, to leave room for the user's self-expression."
6. **Good design is honest.** "It does not make a product more innovative, powerful or valuable than it really is. It does not attempt to manipulate the consumer with promises that cannot be kept."
7. **Good design is long-lasting.** "It avoids being fashionable and therefore never appears antiquated... it lasts many years – even in today's throwaway society."
8. **Good design is thorough down to the last detail.** "Nothing must be arbitrary or left to chance. Care and accuracy in the design process show respect towards the user."
9. **Good design is environmentally-friendly.** "Design makes an important contribution to the preservation of the environment. It conserves resources and minimizes physical and visual pollution throughout the lifecycle of the product."
10. **Good design is as little design as possible.** "Less, but better – because it concentrates on the essential aspects, and the products are not burdened with non-essentials."

## 4. "Weniger, aber besser" / "Less, but better"

- The German phrase **"Weniger, aber besser"** is Rams's core philosophy. (Wikipedia; Design Museum)
- Reframed as opportunity, not constraint: "Less, but better" is "not a constraint, it is an advantage which allows us more space for our real life." (Wallpaper / WDO 2025 coverage and Rams materials)
- The deepest single quote (Gary Hustwit's 2018 *Rams* documentary): **"Indifference towards people and the reality in which they live is actually the one and only cardinal sin in design."** (Hustwit; Wikipedia)
- On obsolescence and waste: "I imagine our current situation will cause future generations to shudder at the thoughtlessness in the way in which we today fill our homes, our cities and our landscape with a chaos of assorted junk." (Vitsœ)
- On eliminating the modish (from the CAFA 2025 retrospective wall text): "As designers we have a great responsibility. I believe designers should eliminate the unnecessary. That means eliminating everything that is modish because this kind of thing is only short-lived." (CAFA)

## 5. Influence on Jony Ive and Apple (the pairing)

- Apple's Jonathan (Jony) Ive was heavily influenced by Rams. Specific homages: **original iPod ↔ Braun T3 radio**; **iOS Calculator ↔ Braun ET66 calculator**; iOS clock app ↔ Braun clocks; the old Podcasts app skeuomorph ↔ Braun TG 60 reel-to-reel. (Wikipedia)
- Ive wrote the **foreword to the 2011 monograph *Dieter Rams: As Little Design as Possible*** (Sophie Lovell, Phaidon). In the foreword he recalls the first Braun product in his parents' home — the **citromatic MPZ 2 juicer**. (9to5Mac; Phaidon; Rams Foundation interview)
- Ive (Rams Foundation interview): "that is what I and so many designers owe Dieter. He articulated the way it should be," and Rams "articulated the framework for the profession that was inspirational and responsible." (rams-foundation.org)
- In Gary Hustwit's **2009 documentary *Objectified*,** Rams said Apple was the only company designing products according to his ten principles for good design. (Search synthesis)
- In **2010 Ive sent Rams an iPhone with a thank-you letter.** (Wikipedia)
