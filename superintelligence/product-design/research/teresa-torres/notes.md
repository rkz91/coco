## Canonical work — Continuous Discovery Habits & the Opportunity Solution Tree

### Definition of continuous discovery (EXACT, verified)
> "at a minimum weekly touchpoints with customers by the team that's building the product where they conduct small research activities in pursuit of a desired product outcome."
Source: https://www.producttalk.org/getting-started-with-discovery/ (also restated across her corpus). This is THE canonical one-sentence definition. Note the three load-bearing parts: (1) weekly cadence, (2) by the trio/team building the product (not a separate research team), (3) in pursuit of a desired *outcome*.

### Opportunity Solution Tree (OST) — four layers (verified)
Source: https://www.producttalk.org/opportunity-solution-trees/
1. **Outcome (root):** a business need / desired outcome. A *product outcome* measures "a customer behavior in the product or a customer's sentiment about the product."
2. **Opportunities:** "Unmet customer need, pain point, or desire" that, if addressed, drives the outcome.
3. **Solutions:** "A product, a feature, a service, a workflow, a process, documentation, or anything else that we offer to customers to help address a known opportunity."
4. **Assumption Tests (experiments):** methods to evaluate which solutions best create customer value while driving business value.
- Recommends updating the opportunity space "every three to four customer interviews."
- "The goal is to focus on your target opportunity" — resist broad solution brainstorming; resist overreacting to individual feedback ("Is this the most important area for us to focus on right now?").

### Story-based interviewing technique (verified)
Source: https://www.producttalk.org/getting-started-with-discovery/
Collect "specific stories about past behavior," not speculative opinion. Example: instead of "what do you like to watch?" ask "Tell me about the last time you watched Netflix." Grounds answers in concrete past behavior. (This is her "excavate the story" technique.)

### Outcomes vs Outputs (verified, recent)
Source: https://www.producttalk.org/outcomes-vs-outputs/ — **dated 2026-03-18**.
- Outcome definition: **"a change in customer behavior or a business result."** Business outcomes = financial metrics (revenue, cost); product outcomes = customer behavior/sentiment in the product.
- Quote: **"Building something isn't the same as creating value. That's the core of this distinction."**
- Quote: **"When we task teams with delivering outputs, they're done when the software ships. When we task teams with delivering outcomes, they aren't done until the software ships _and_ has the expected impact."**

## JTBD camp tension (CORRECTED ASSUMPTION — important)

The prompt framed Torres vs Ulwick + Moesta as "JTBD-vs-continuous-discovery camps." **This is a real and productive tension, but the framing needs nuance and I corrected it:**

- Torres is **NOT anti-JTBD**. She considers Jobs to Be Done "an effective framework for uncovering customer needs, pain points and desires" and deliberately defines discovery to be **framework-agnostic** because "different tools and frameworks resonate with different teams." (Source: https://www.mindtheproduct.com/teresa-torres-answers-your-product-discovery-questions/)
- The real productive conflict is **methodological**, three distinct camps:
  - **Torres:** continuous, team-run, weekly *generative* discovery organized around an opportunity space; outcome-anchored; lightweight and habit-based. Her "opportunity" is a need/pain/desire framed in the customer's words.
  - **Tony Ulwick (Outcome-Driven Innovation):** rigorous, *quantitative*, up-front. Jobs decomposed into ~50–150 measurable "desired outcome" statements, surveyed and prioritized by opportunity score (importance × satisfaction gap). Ulwick has publicly argued that vague "needs" language and unstructured interviewing (the camp he associates with the Christensen/Moesta lineage and, by extension, looser discovery practice) is unreliable. Source: https://strategyn.com/jobs-to-be-done/ and https://jobs-to-be-done.com/the-history-of-jobs-to-be-done-and-outcome-driven-innovation-a2fdfd0c7a9a
  - **Bob Moesta (Switch/demand-side JTBD):** the "switch interview" — interview people who *just* bought/switched, reconstruct the timeline and the forces (push/pull/anxiety/habit) that caused the switch. Demand-side, story-reconstruction, sales-adjacent. Source: https://jobs-to-be-done.com/
- The genuine tension: Ulwick distrusts the looseness of generative interviewing and wants quantified outcome statements; Moesta centers the *switch moment* and causal forces rather than an ongoing opportunity tree; Torres centers *continuous habit + outcome alignment* over any single heavyweight upfront study. All three would disagree about how much structure, how much quantification, and where the unit of analysis sits (opportunity vs desired-outcome-statement vs switch). This is the Torres↔Ulwick↔Moesta three-camp tension the ROSTER.md explicitly wires (see ROSTER.md line 10).

## 2025–2026 AI pivot (MAJOR recent shift — well documented)

This is the dominant recent signal and must be foregrounded. In **March 2025 Torres started "vibe coding"** and by 2026 reports spending ~60% of her time on real engineering — building AI-powered discovery tooling (notably AI-generated opportunity solution trees for Vistaly). Her stance is **augmentation, not replacement**, with human-in-the-loop synthesis.

### AI in discovery — Aakash Gupta podcast (dated 2025-08-12)
Source: https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/teresa-torres-podcast (also https://aakashgupta.medium.com/teresa-torres-masterclass-on-ai-discovery-feae80a3847f)
- **"AI summaries can miss 20–40% of important detail."** Keep humans in the loop to "validate, correct, and preserve empathy." Use AI to "augment your workflow, not replace it."
- AI prototyping: avoid the **"Homer Simpson car"** problem (cramming every feature). Prototype "only the specific element that tests your riskiest assumption."
- Reaffirms: live customer interviews weekly; "excavate the story" technique for chronological behavioral detail.

### Behind the Scenes: Building AI-Generated Opportunity Solution Trees (dated 2026-05-13)
Source: https://www.producttalk.org/behind-the-scenes-ai-osts/
- Engineering sprint building AI OSTs for Vistaly. 8 design partners used the prototype; rebuilding for production revealed the prototype was only ~20% of production-ready functionality ("demo vs product" lesson, mirrors Karpathy's works.any/works.all).
- **Quote:** "We want you to engage with them, correct them, collaborate with the AI. We want to scaffold cross-interview synthesis, not do it for you."
- AI struggles with: data integrity across tree splits/merges, step-by-step explanations matching outputs, reliable output without verification loops. Solved via an **agentic self-correction loop** — errors dropped from ~50% of runs to near-zero.
- AI trees are **recommendations requiring validation**, not authoritative outputs.

### Vibe Coding Best Practices (dated 2026-04-01)
Source: https://www.producttalk.org/vibe-coding-best-practices/
- Two feedback loops: **Plan-Review-Fix** (clarify requirements in markdown before coding) and **Implement-Review-Fix** (AI code reviewers catch agent mistakes).
- **Quote:** "If you aren't sure what you want or you change your mind, you can end up with a lot of messy code."
- **Quote:** "The clearer you are about what you want, the better output you'll get from the agent." (Product thinking = a technical skill now.)
- The "doom loop": when requirements shift mid-coding, data/controller/view layers desync into intractable bugs; restarting with clear requirements is often faster than fixing tech debt.
- Code review priorities: error handling, test coverage, security (dependencies, secrets, input validation, CORS).

### My Team of Agents (dated 2026-05-20)
Source: https://www.producttalk.org/my-team-of-agents/
- Three specialized agents (podcast manager, sales admin, coding manager) via scheduled tasks on a Mac Mini, within existing subscription costs.
- **Quote:** "After hearing one too many horror stories about hours wasted and surprise bills, I wanted a safer way to explore the advantages of OpenClaw while also managing the costs."
- Philosophy: compartmentalize agent capability (task folders, identity docs, scheduled scripts) over broad unrestricted machine access. Cautious + practical.

## Blind spots (synthesized, defensible)
- Her model assumes a stable, empowered **product trio** (PM + designer + engineer) with weekly access to customers — fragile in agencies, heavily regulated B2B, hardware, or low-trust orgs where customer access is gated.
- Weekly-touchpoint cadence can become **ritual/theater** (interviewing to hit the cadence rather than to reduce a real risk) — a critique her own ODI/quant critics raise.
- Lighter on **quantitative prioritization and statistical rigor** than the Ulwick/Kohavi camps — opportunity sizing in the OST is qualitative judgment.
- The 2025–2026 AI/engineering pivot is genuine but recent; her vibe-coding guidance is practitioner-grade, not security/architecture-grade — she defers to engineers on the hard parts (consistent with her own framing).
