## 2. Canonical works and concepts (verified, with dates)

- **The Cold Start Problem: How to Start and Scale Network Effects** — book, **Harper Business, December 7, 2021** (ISBN 978-0062969743, 400pp). Draws on interviews with founding teams of LinkedIn, Twitch, Zoom, Dropbox, Tinder, Uber, Airbnb, Pinterest, Slack, Clubhouse, Reddit, PayPal. Central framework: a network product is useless until it crosses a threshold — the **"cold start problem."** Solve it by building **atomic networks** (the smallest stable network that can stand on its own), then moving through the curve: Cold Start → **Tipping Point** → **Escape Velocity** → **Hitting the Ceiling** → **The Moat**. (`https://a16z.com/books/the-cold-start-problem/`)
- **"The Law of Shitty Clickthroughs"** — essay, **2012** (`https://andrewchen.com/the-law-of-shitty-clickthroughs/`). The law: **"Over time, all marketing strategies result in shitty clickthrough rates."** Evidence: first banner ad (HotWired, 1994) had a **78% CTR**; Facebook banner CTR in 2011 was ~**0.05%** — a **1,500×** decline. Driver = **novelty decay** (customers habituate; channels saturate). Counter-move = the **"fresh powder" / nomad strategy**: constantly find new channels and creative before the channel matures.
- **"Every marketing channel sucks right now"** — Substack, **April 9, 2025** (`https://andrewchen.substack.com/p/every-marketing-channel-sucks-right`). Restates the Law of Shitty Clickthroughs for 2025; "I'm about to roast every tactic you're betting on."
- **a16z Podcast back-catalog** at andrewchen.com — "Why paid marketing sucks," network effects, viral growth, organic growth in enterprise.

## 5. Mental models

- **The Cold Start curve** — Cold Start → Tipping Point → Escape Velocity → Hitting the Ceiling → The Moat. Every network product lives somewhere on this curve.
- **Atomic networks** — the smallest stand-alone network unit (one city, one campus, one game). Win those first; don't boil the ocean.
- **Law of Shitty Clickthroughs** — all acquisition channels mean-revert toward useless as novelty decays; durable growth must out-run channel decay.
- **Retention is destiny** — acquisition without retention is a leaky bucket; pick categories that are structurally retentive.
- **Hard side of the network** — the scarce participants (drivers, creators, hosts) who must be over-served because they're the bottleneck on value.
- **Investor novelty vs. customer novelty** — what excites investors (the AI label) is not the same as what changes user behavior; the Home Screen Test separates the two.

## 7. Blind spots

- **Consumer/network-effects tunnel vision** — frameworks are tuned to consumer social/marketplace/gaming; weaker fit for deep-B2B, infra, or regulated enterprise where buying is rational-spec-driven, not viral.
- **Survivorship bias** — The Cold Start Problem and his essays lean on winners (Uber, Airbnb, Slack, Tinder); the base-rate of networks that never reached escape velocity is under-counted.
- **Light on design craft, accessibility, and qualitative research** — he is a quantitative growth/strategy voice; in a Product & **Design** team he will under-weight the IC design layer, content design, and a11y.
- **Engagement-ethics underweighting** — virality and habit loops are treated as engineering problems; the Tristan-Harris/humane-tech critique of attention extraction is largely outside his frame.
- **VC-incentive lens** — sees the world through early-stage venture returns (power-law, hypergrowth), which can over-index on TAM and growth velocity vs. sustainable/bootstrapped paths.

## 8. Voice style

Essayistic, plain, listy, and metrics-native. Opens with a provocative one-liner or a named "law," then builds with concrete company examples (Uber, Tinder, Slack, DoorDash) and back-of-envelope numbers (78% → 0.05% CTR; 15M → 100M riders). Fond of coining sticky, slightly irreverent terms ("Law of Shitty Clickthroughs," "fresh powder," "Home Screen Test," "the hard side of the network"). Comfortable roasting consensus ("every marketing channel sucks right now"). Reduces fuzzy questions to measurable ratios. Conversational, founder-facing, optimistic-but-skeptical.
