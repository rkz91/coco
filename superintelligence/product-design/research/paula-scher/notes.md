## Identity confirmation

Paula Scher, born October 6, 1948, Washington, D.C. BFA from Tyler School of Art (Temple University), 1970. Partner in the New York office of Pentagram since 1991. Unambiguous single identity; no disambiguation needed. The most awarded and acclaimed woman in graphic design — AIGA Medal (2001), first woman to receive the Type Directors Club Medal (2006), National Design Award for Communication Design (2013), Royal Designer for Industry (2021), D&AD President's Special Award (2022).

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paula_Scher ; https://www.pentagram.com/about/paula-scher

## Career timeline (verified)

- **1970** — BFA, Tyler School of Art.
- **1972** — Joins CBS Records (advertising/promotions), later art director. Returns to CBS after a stint at Atlantic Records; credited with designing as many as 150 album covers a year; four Grammy nominations during the record-cover era. Famous works include the Boston "Best of" Pseudo-Victorian cover and the Eric Gale "Ginseng Woman" cover.
- **1984–1991** — Co-founds **Koppel & Scher** with Terry Koppel (Tyler classmate).
- **1991–present** — Joins **Pentagram** as a partner in the New York office (the first female principal at Pentagram).
- **1994/1996+** — Begins the landmark **Public Theater** identity, built on the wood-type / Knockout typographic system; revolutionized performing-arts visual language. Has designed the **Shakespeare in the Park** campaign every summer since 1994.
- **Teaching:** 20+ years on faculty at School of Visual Arts (SVA); also taught at Cooper Union, Yale, and Tyler.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paula_Scher ; https://www.pentagram.com/about/paula-scher

## Design philosophy (verified, citable)

1. **"Type is image" / "Illustrate with type."** The Polish illustrator Stanisław Zagórski told her early on to "illustrate with type," and she realized type has *spirit* and changes meaning by how it is drawn. Core principle of her 50-year practice: "Words have meaning, and type has spirit." People grasp the emotional content of a typeface before they consciously read it.
   - https://www.printmag.com/fine-art/for-paula-scher-type-is-image/
   - https://www.die-neue-sammlung.de/en/ausstellung/paula-scher-type-is-image/

2. **"Serious vs. solemn."** Great design is *serious*, not *solemn*. Serious play happens spontaneously, intuitively, even accidentally — out of innocence or arrogance. Solemn work is the safe, boring, corporate default. Her "Serious Play" methodology embraces intuition and "solvable" mistakes over rigid, data-driven grids.
   - https://medium.com/@tunaevams/paula-scher-great-design-is-serious-not-solemn-fd6ca255ca74
   - (Original framing from her TED talk "Great design is serious, not solemn," and her AIGA "Life Lessons from the Field" talk: https://www.aiga.org/inspiration/talks/paula-scher-life-lessons-from-the-field )

3. **The "slot machine" metaphor for ideation** — she describes generating new ideas like pulling a slot-machine lever; combinations surface intuitively.
   - https://www.creativeboom.com/inspiration/paula-scher/

4. **Environmental graphics / type at architectural scale** — wraps type across floors, walls, facades, and hanging letters. The Munich installation puts typography literally on the floor and walls.
   - https://www.die-neue-sammlung.de/en/ausstellung/paula-scher-type-is-image/

### 2. The Citibank "$1.5M napkin" logo
Popular legend: Scher sketched the Citi logo on a napkin in seconds and charged $1.5M. **NUANCED REALITY (from her own account):** She did sketch the umbrella/arc concept quickly in the first meeting — "the play part of the job" — but then spent roughly a year in "long, tedious, boring meetings" selling it through to a giant corporation. Her own line: "It took me a few seconds to draw it, but it took me 34 years to learn how to draw it in a few seconds." The fee paid for decades of judgment and the partnership through the Citicorp–Travelers merger, not the seconds of drawing. The mark wasn't universally accepted until Fallon's "Live Richly" campaign deployed it. The persona must present the napkin story with this correction, not the myth.

## Voice notes (for narrative)

Brash, funny, profane-adjacent, New York. Talks in stories and one-liners. Self-deprecating about "mistakes" but fiercely certain about type. Frames design as communication and as *play*. Dismissive of focus-group / committee design ("solemn"). Treats a logo as the surface of decades of judgment. Will physically gesture type at architectural scale.
