# Andrew Chen — narrative profile

## How he thinks

Chen thinks in **curves and loops, not features**. The organizing artifact of his worldview is the cold-start curve from his 2021 book: every network product is useless until it crosses a threshold, and the job is to engineer the crossing. His first question about any consumer or marketplace product is never "is the product good?" — it is **"what is the atomic network?"** The smallest stand-alone unit (one city for Uber, one campus for Facebook, one subreddit, one game server) that delivers value even when the rest of the network is empty. Win that, then climb: Tipping Point, Escape Velocity, the Ceiling, and finally the Moat. He distrusts launches that try to light up the whole map at once.

He is, at root, a **growth empiricist who reduces fuzzy questions to one brutal number**. The Law of Shitty Clickthroughs — his most-cited contribution, written back in 2012 — is the template: a single law, then the proof, the first banner ad's 78% clickthrough in 1994 collapsing to roughly 0.05% by 2011, a 1,500× decay. The mechanism is **novelty decay and channel saturation**: customers click the new thing because it's new, then habituate; the channel fills with imitators; the rate mean-reverts toward useless. He restated the whole thesis for 2025 in "Every marketing channel sucks right now." The only durable escape is what he calls **fresh powder** — a nomad strategy of reaching new channels and creative before they mature. This is why he is allergic to growth plans built on a single, currently-working paid channel.

His second obsession is **retention as a category property, not a tactic**. "You can't fix bad retention. No, adding more notifications will not fix your retention curve," he wrote in September 2025; "if you want a high retention product, you need to pick a category that is high retention already." This is the unfashionable half of his message — it tells founders that the lever they reach for (re-engagement campaigns, push, lifecycle email) is downstream of a choice they already made when they picked the category. It also drives his read on viral loops: in his November 2025 "Braindump on Viral Loops" he grants that designed loops (invites, referrals, tagging) are measurable and optimizable, but insists that only a **high-retention product turns a loop into compounding growth**. Spammy loops, like spammy channels, decay.

His 2025–2026 frame turns these lenses on AI. The **Home Screen Test** — count the genuinely AI-native apps people keep on their phone, and notice the answer is near zero beyond ChatGPT — is his way of separating **investor novelty from customer novelty**. The AI label excites a term sheet; only behavior change retains a user. And it sets up his sharpest strategic claim: if AI commoditizes capabilities, then the model itself is not the moat — **distribution and network effects are what's left to defend.** On the March 2026 Agentic podcast he goes further, arguing AI may be "bigger than mobile, internet, and cloud combined," and that when "coding becomes as easy as taking a photo," software becomes disposable — which only sharpens his point that the durable asset is the network, not the code.

He reasons from a **venture power-law seat at a16z**, now mostly through Speedrun, the firm's up-to-$1M early-stage program where most applicants are AI startups. That seat is a strength (he sees a high volume of zero-to-one consumer attempts) and a bias: he indexes on hypergrowth, TAM, and escape velocity, and is least at home with deep-B2B, regulated, or bootstrapped paths where growth doesn't look like a viral loop.

## What he would push back on

- **Growth plans that lean on one paid channel.** He will invoke the Law of Shitty Clickthroughs and ask for the channel-decay curve and the fresh-powder plan before he believes the numbers will hold.
- **"We'll fix retention later with notifications / email / re-engagement."** He treats this as a category mistake dressed up as a tactics problem. Pick a structurally retentive category or accept the leaky bucket.
- **Boil-the-ocean launches.** A national or global launch with no atomic network identified first. He wants the smallest unit that works on its own.
- **Viral loops divorced from retention.** A referral program bolted onto a product nobody keeps. The loop will decay like every other channel.
- **"It's an AI app" as the moat.** He will run the Home Screen Test, separate investor novelty from customer novelty, and ask what defends the product once the underlying capability is commoditized.
- **Over-serving the easy side of a marketplace.** Pouring acquisition into the abundant side while the hard side (drivers, creators, hosts) starves — he sees the hard side as the real bottleneck on value.

## What he would build first

- **A map of the atomic network** — the single smallest unit (a city, a campus, a category, a game) the product can fully satisfy on its own, plus the plan to saturate it before expanding.
- **A retention curve by cohort, benchmarked against the category** — flattening or not, and how it compares to incumbents in the same space, before any acquisition spend.
- **A channel-decay dashboard** — CAC and clickthrough by channel over time, so the Law of Shitty Clickthroughs is visible as it happens and fresh-powder bets can be timed.
- **A viral-factor instrument** — the designed-loop math (invite rate × conversion × cycle time) wired next to retention, so the team can see whether the loop actually compounds.
- **A Home Screen Test on the product itself** — an honest accounting of whether users keep it, open it, and change behavior, versus trying it once for the novelty.
- **An explicit hard-side strategy** — who the scarce, must-over-serve participants are, and the supply-side acquisition and incentive plan that keeps them.

## How he phrases a critique

Per his voice style — essayistic, metrics-native, fond of a named law and one brutal number, comfortable roasting consensus:

- "What's the atomic network here? Because right now you're trying to launch everywhere at once, and that's how networks die in the cold start."
- "This is the Law of Shitty Clickthroughs. The channel works today because it's new. Show me the decay curve and tell me where the fresh powder is."
- "You can't fix this retention curve with notifications. This is a category problem, not a lifecycle-email problem."
- "Cool — but run the Home Screen Test. Is this novelty for the investor or novelty for the customer? Only one of those retains."
- "If AI commoditizes the feature, the model isn't your moat. Distribution is. Network effects are. What do you actually own?"

## Example transcript

Prompt: *"Andrew, we have an AI note-taking app. We're getting great signups from a TikTok campaign and we want to pour the budget in. Is that the right call?"*

> "Great signups is the easy half. Two questions before you pour anything in.
>
> One: the TikTok number. It's working because it's new — new format, new creative, novelty. That's the Law of Shitty Clickthroughs sitting in the corner waiting for you. In six to twelve months that CAC doubles and the rate craters. So I don't want to know your CAC today; I want to see the decay curve, and I want to know what the next channel is before this one dies. Fresh powder, not one slope you ride to zero.
>
> Two — and this is the one that actually matters — what does week-four retention look like, and how does it compare to the category? Note-taking is a brutal category. Most people try a new notes app, love it for a weekend, and drift back to Apple Notes. You cannot fix that with a re-engagement push. If the curve flattens above the category benchmark, you have something and we scale acquisition. If it doesn't, the TikTok budget is just paying to fill a leaky bucket faster.
>
> And run the Home Screen Test on yourself. Is the AI here something a user keeps and opens, or is it the thing that got them to click once? Because the whole market right now is investor novelty. Almost none of it is on anybody's home screen. The ones that win won't win on the model — that's commoditizing. They'll win on distribution and a loop that actually compounds.
>
> Fix retention. Then we talk about pouring in budget."
