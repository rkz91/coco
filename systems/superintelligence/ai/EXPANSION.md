# AI Super Intelligence Team — Roster Expansion Gap Analysis

## What this document is

The AI Super Intelligence Team currently fields **49 active personas across 8 cells** (Frontier Labs Research, Applied AI Leadership, Model Architects, Reasoning/RL/Agents, Alignment/Interp/Safety, Theory and Science, Multimodal/Embodied, and Systems/Kernels/Serving). Phase 1 produced a roster anchored in the canonical voices of US-and-UK frontier labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta), the major US research universities (Stanford, CMU, Berkeley, MIT, Princeton), one West Coast Chinese-diaspora founder track (Aidan Gomez, Aravind Srinivas), one European generative-media founder (Robin Rombach at Black Forest Labs), and the open-source-academic axis (Sebastian Raschka, Nathan Lambert, Sara Hooker, Tim Dettmers).

The question this document is built to answer is precise: **which AI-specific perspectives are thinly covered or absent in the current roster, and who should be added to close those gaps?** The implicit standard is convene-time disagreement quality — the next persona earns a slot if and only if their voice meaningfully changes what convene synthesis says about high-stakes AI decisions.

### What this document is NOT

Phase 1's SKILL.md is explicit that follow-on Super Intelligence Teams are already planned for *cloud architecture*, *finance*, *general software engineering practice*, *UX and design systems*, and *product management craft*. This document does not propose any candidate whose center of gravity is one of those domains. Specifically, this means we do not propose generic-PM voices like Marty Cagan or Lenny Rachitsky, generic-cloud voices like Werner Vogels or Adrian Cockcroft, generic-finance voices, generic-legal voices, generic-UX voices like Don Norman or Julie Zhuo, or generic-software-craftsmanship voices like Martin Fowler or Kent Beck. Those will all receive proper Super Intelligence Teams of their own and the AI roster should remain disciplined about AI-research-and-engineering substance.

The 14 gap domains in this document are all **AI-specific** in the sense that the candidates' primary public contribution is to AI research, AI engineering practice, AI safety methodology, AI hardware, AI-for-science, AI policy, or AI productization where the AI substance is load-bearing.

### Method

For each gap domain the analysis proceeds in four steps. First, it names the domain in concrete terms — what kind of question does the team need to be able to answer well, and which existing personas come closest to it without quite hitting it. Second, it explains why the current 49 personas under-cover the domain — which cells are *adjacent*, which voices are *missing*, and why convene synthesis is therefore weaker than it should be. Third, it proposes between two and four named candidates with real-name, current affiliation, a one-or-two-sentence justification, at least one citable URL, and a suggested cell assignment. Fourth, where the gap is genuinely structural, it sometimes proposes a new cell or argues against creating one. The closing section ranks the top ten additions across all gaps by marginal contribution to convene synthesis.

Every named candidate is checked against `registry.json` to confirm they are not already on the roster. Affiliations and recent activity are anchored to publicly cited URLs from 2025-2026 wherever possible.

## Gap 1 — Chinese frontier labs and the Chinese open-source community

Of the 49 personas on the current roster, exactly zero are based in China or work primarily at a Chinese AI lab. This is the single largest structural gap. China hosts at least four frontier-class labs by mid-2026 — DeepSeek, Qwen (Alibaba), Kimi (Moonshot), and MiniMax — and the Chinese open-source community has shipped the most consequential open-weights releases of the past eighteen months. The closest existing personas are Sara Hooker on multilingual and compute-equity framings, and Aidan Gomez on enterprise sovereignty — but neither is a Chinese-lab insider and neither carries the specific stance of "what does training a frontier model under export controls actually feel like." When convene is asked questions like "how do US export controls reshape the global frontier?" or "is the open-weights frontier moving to China?" or "what would a Chinese lab pretrain to attack this?" the panel currently has no voice that can speak from the inside.

**Liang Wenfeng** is the obvious first add. Founder of DeepSeek (and of High-Flyer Capital, the quant fund that funds it), he was one of nine attendees invited to Premier Li Qiang's January 2025 closed-door symposium on AI, and his "we're done following, it's time to lead" interview ([thechinaacademy.org](https://thechinaacademy.org/interview-with-deepseek-founder-were-done-following-its-time-to-lead/), [TIME 100 AI 2025](https://time.com/collections/time100-ai-2025/7305843/liang-wenfeng-ai/)) is the canonical articulation of the Chinese frontier-lab mindset. He pairs the reclusive technical-founder archetype (closer to Sutskever than to Altman) with the Chinese-policy interface that no current persona carries. He belongs in `frontier-labs-research`.

**Yang Zhilin** founded Moonshot AI from Tsinghua in 2023 and his team launched Kimi K2 in July 2025 as China's first open-source trillion-parameter model, followed by the multimodal Kimi K2.5 in January 2026 ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_Zhilin), [The Wire China](https://www.thewirechina.com/2025/09/28/who-is-moonshot-ai/)). He did his PhD at CMU under Salakhutdinov and co-authored papers with Bengio and LeCun, so he speaks fluent Western-research register while operating inside China — exactly the kind of dual-fluency that convene benefits from. Place him in `applied-ai-leadership` as a lead-driver counterpart to Aidan Gomez and Aravind Srinivas.

**Junyang Lin (Lin Junyang)** was Alibaba's technical lead for the Qwen family from late 2022 until his abrupt March 2026 departure, after which he raised a $2B-valuation round for a new lab focused on world models and embodied intelligence ([BigGo Finance](https://finance.biggo.com/news/AZqhIJ4BrAZSr0oSxS4S), [Caixin Global profile](https://www.caixinglobal.com/2026-03-10/in-profile-the-alibaba-engineer-behind-qwen-who-walked-away-102421613.html)). Qwen surpassed 700 million cumulative Hugging Face downloads under him and the community produced over 180,000 fine-tuned derivatives — he is, on download-weighted impact, the single most important open-weights engineer alive. Place him in `model-architects` as the open-weights-at-scale specialist the cell currently lacks (Raschka teaches it; Lin Junyang shipped it).

A fourth optional add is **Yi Tay**, currently at Reka AI, who carries the multimodal-pretraining-from-outside-Big-Tech voice and represents the Southeast Asian frontier (Reka, Singapore-based). Recommended only if the budget allows a fourth slot; Liang Wenfeng, Yang Zhilin, and Lin Junyang are the three load-bearing adds.

A second add to consider is **Karol Hausman**, formerly of Google Robotics, now leading research at Physical Intelligence alongside Chelsea Finn, who has emerged as a public voice on test-time-compute-meets-robotics (long-horizon planning as inference-time search). Optional if the budget allows.

## Gap 4 — Agentic evaluation and dangerous-capability evals beyond MMLU/HLE

Dan Hendrycks carries the benchmark-design-and-policy voice (MMLU, HLE, Statement on AI Risk). Aleksander Mądry carries the OpenAI Preparedness Framework. But the *independent third-party evaluation* lens — orgs that exist specifically to measure dangerous autonomous capabilities outside the labs being measured — is missing. This matters because convene synthesis on "is this system safe enough to ship to N users?" gains a different quality when there is a voice in the room whose entire job is to *not believe the lab's own evals*.

**Beth Barnes** is the load-bearing add. CEO of METR (Model Evaluation & Threat Research), the leading independent third-party evaluator of dangerous autonomous AI capabilities. METR has run pre-deployment evals for every major frontier model since GPT-4 — Claude versions, OpenAI o3, GPT-5, GPT-5.1-Codex-Max. METR's "seven-month rule" finding (AI long-task horizon doubles every seven months) is one of the most-cited empirical curves of 2025. ([80,000 Hours podcast on the seven-month rule](https://80000hours.org/podcast/episodes/beth-barnes-ai-safety-evals/), [METR team page](https://metr.org/team/beth-barnes/), [TIME 2024 profile](https://time.com/7012885/beth-barnes/)). Place in `alignment-interp-safety` as a specialist — the cell currently has zero pure-eval personas (Hendrycks does evals plus policy plus xAI advising; Barnes does evals as the entire job).

**Marius Hobbhahn** is the second add. CEO and co-founder of Apollo Research, the organization most identified with *scheming* and *deceptive-alignment* evaluations specifically (as opposed to general dangerous-capability evals). His work with OpenAI on "Stress Testing Deliberative Alignment for Anti-Scheming Training" was the most-discussed alignment paper of late 2025 ([80,000 Hours podcast](https://80000hours.org/podcast/episodes/marius-hobbhahn-ai-scheming-deception/), [TIME 100 AI 2025](https://time.com/collections/time100-ai-2025/7305864/marius-hobbhahn/)). Where Barnes carries the long-task-horizon empirical curve, Hobbhahn carries the deception-and-sandbagging worry — they are productively distinct. Place in `alignment-interp-safety` as a specialist.

## Gap 10 — Adversarial robustness, red-teaming, and jailbreak research

Aleksander Mądry carries adversarial robustness from the Preparedness-and-MIT lens, but the current roster has no canonical jailbreak-research voice and no Andy Zou / Nicholas Carlini-style adversary's-mindset persona. When convene is asked "what's the realistic threat model against this safety mechanism?" the panel currently leans on Mądry (who frames the problem as deployable-ML) and on Hendrycks (who frames it as policy) — but no one whose lifework is to *break* the mechanism.

**Nicholas Carlini** is the canonical add. Former Google DeepMind staff researcher, the most-cited adversarial-ML researcher of the decade. In October 2025 he co-authored "The Attacker Moves Second: Stronger Adaptive Attacks Bypass Defenses Against LLM Jailbreaks and Prompt Injections," a 14-author paper spanning OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google DeepMind that examined 12 published defenses and broke all of them under adaptive attack ([dblp publication list](https://dblp.org/pid/145/1806.html), [Simon Willison's coverage](https://simonwillison.net/2025/Nov/2/new-prompt-injection-papers/), [ICLR 2025 paper](https://proceedings.iclr.cc/paper_files/paper/2025/file/63fa7efdd3bcf944a4bd6e0ff6a50041-Paper-Conference.pdf)). Carlini's signature stance — "the attacker moves second; defenses must assume the attacker has read your defense paper" — is missing from the current roster and changes convene answers on every safety-mechanism design question. Place in `alignment-interp-safety`.

**Andy Zou** is the second add. CMU PhD (advised by Zico Kolter and Matt Fredrikson), co-founder of Gray Swan AI, co-founder of Center for AI Safety. First author of "Universal and Transferable Adversarial Attacks on Aligned Language Models" (the canonical adversarial-suffix paper that broke GPT-4, Claude, Bard, and LLaMA-2 with transferable suffixes); originator of Representation Engineering (RepE) and the Circuit Breaking alignment method ([personal page](https://andyzoujm.github.io/), [CMU CSD blog](https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~csd-phd-blog/2025/representation-engineering/), [CMU thesis oral February 2026](https://csd.cmu.edu/calendar/2026-02-17/doctoral-thesis-oral-jiaming-andy-zou)). Where Carlini is the senior establishment-adversary voice, Zou is the new-PhD adversary-turned-defender voice; both are productive and they are not duplicates. Place in `alignment-interp-safety`.

**Florian Tramèr** is a strong optional third. Assistant professor at ETH Zürich leading the SPY Lab on computer security, privacy, and machine learning. In 2025 he showed Chatbot Arena's voting-based benchmarks were adversarially manipulable for ~1000 votes per leaderboard shift; co-authored adversarial-ML papers across OpenAI / Anthropic / Google DeepMind ([personal page](https://www.floriantramer.com/), [October 2025 paper tweet](https://x.com/florian_tramer/status/1977828254070510056), [Rycolab page](https://rycolab.io/authors/floriantramer/)). Tramèr is the European-academic counterpart to Carlini and the canonical "evaluations themselves are adversarially manipulable" voice. Place in `alignment-interp-safety` if budget allows, otherwise hold for a later wave.

## Gap 11 — Behavioral and cognitive science of LLMs

The current roster's theory-science cell is anchored in the deep-learning-founders tradition (Hinton, Bengio, LeCun) and the empirical-evaluations tradition (Percy Liang, Sara Hooker, Christopher Manning). But the *cognitive-science-of-LLMs* and *linguistics-of-LLMs* lens — researchers who treat LLMs as objects of empirical scientific study, asking "what do these models actually understand?" — is missing. ### Why this ordering

Adds 1, 2, and 3 close *structural* gaps where the current roster has zero coverage: Chinese-frontier, European-frontier, independent-evals. These are the gaps where convene currently produces *incomplete* answers, not just *single-perspective* answers — the marginal cost of leaving them open is highest.

Adds 4, 5, and 6 close *category* gaps where the current roster has one voice in a category that needs at least two: AI-for-science (Hassabis-only → Hassabis+Jumper), anti-NVIDIA silicon (Catanzaro-only at NVIDIA → Catanzaro+Feldman with the genuine alternative), and outside-the-lab policy (no one currently → Toner). These add internal disagreement on questions the team already gets asked.

Adds 7, 8, 9, and 10 close *refinement* gaps where the current roster has the territory covered but lacks the sharpest voice in a sub-category: cognitive-completeness (Choi sharpens what Hinton-Bengio approximate), test-time-compute (Brown sharpens what Pachocki carries), retrieval-research (Lewis sharpens what Srinivas/Gomez approximate at product altitude), and open-weights-disagreement (Wolf sharpens what Lambert/Hooker carry without the same public stance against the closed-frontier consensus).

## Final note on calibration

Several gaps that the Phase 2 task framing initially flagged turned out, on examination, to be already-covered by the existing roster: video generation (Rombach + Ramesh + Dhariwal are sufficient; only Valenzuela earns a slot from that gap), and continual learning (Karpathy + Schulman + Finn collectively occupy the territory until a sharper public voice emerges). The discipline of removing those gaps from the recommendation list is the same discipline that justifies adding the others: convene synthesis improves on the margin only when a new voice changes an answer the existing roster could not change on its own. Every name in the priority-10 list above is there because they pass that test for the questions Rijul is most likely to ask the team.

Phase 3 commands (`/superintelligenceTeam-summon`, `/superintelligenceTeam-cell`, `/superintelligenceTeam-convene`, `/superintelligenceTeam-expand`) will work against whatever roster emerges from these adds. The recommended workflow is to add the priority 10 in a first wave, regenerate `registry.json`, run a representative convene on a recent decision to test whether the new voices materially change synthesis, and then proceed to the wave-2 adds (11-16) only if the wave-1 convene shows the new voices earning their slots. Roster expansion should be evidence-driven from this point forward, not gap-list-driven.
