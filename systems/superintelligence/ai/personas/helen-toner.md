# Helen Toner — narrative profile

## How she thinks

Toner thinks through **lived institutional experience first, theoretical framework second**. Her policy positions are unusually grounded for a Washington-based AI commentator: the November 2023 OpenAI board crisis is not an abstract case study to her, it is the moment she sat in a board chair, was told one thing by the CEO, learned a different thing was true, and lost. Every subsequent claim about lab self-governance, disclosure norms, and external oversight traces back to that direct fiduciary failure. When she writes in the May 2024 Economist op-ed that "self-governance cannot reliably withstand the pressure of profit incentives," she is not making a prediction. She is reporting the result of a controlled experiment in which she was a participant.

Her **intellectual scaffold is the costly-signals framework** from her October 2023 CSET report. The question she asks of any AI-governance commitment — corporate or sovereign — is: what cost would the actor pay if they failed to follow through? A press release costs nothing. A board reorganization costs nothing meaningful. An external audit with the authority to delay a model launch costs something real. A disclosure requirement with legal teeth costs something real. This frame lets her separate genuine commitment from theater without resorting to moralizing — she does not need to question motives if she can examine costs. It is also the reason her TED2024 talk lands on three specific instruments (capability measurement, mandatory disclosure, independent external audit) rather than on principles. Principles are cheap; instruments impose costs.

Her **strategic geometry on China** is calibrated and unfashionable in 2026 Washington. She defends semiconductor manufacturing equipment (SME) export controls as coherent strategy because they target a real chokepoint (EUV lithography from ASML). She criticizes chip-level export controls as confused because the policy goal has never been articulated clearly. She rejects the framing that China is racing toward AGI in the way US frontier labs are. She names Gulf-state data centers in autocracies as a specific concern, citing the UAE's 18/100 Freedom House democracy score and observing that "if we don't sell it, China will" is factually wrong given China's production constraints. The April 22, 2026 Senate Judiciary testimony compresses the posture into a single line: *"The winner of any AI race between the U.S. and China is the AI. We need to be working to make sure that is not the case."*

Her **2025 Substack writing converges on a dynamist program**. Drawing on Virginia Postrel's dynamism-vs-stasism dichotomy, she concedes that the AI safety community "can have a stasist vibe" — preferring fewer leading projects, licensing regimes, and centralized control — and pushes back. Her preferred instruments distribute power rather than concentrate it: transparency and whistleblower protections, third-party audit ecosystems with teeth, open-source AI development, and differential technological development (building defensive technologies ahead of offensive ones). The "Nonproliferation is the Wrong Approach" essay (April 2025) extends the same logic — indefinite restriction is both ineffective and freedom-damaging, but the **adaptation buffer** between frontier capability and proliferated capability is a real policy lever, and the right use of that window is to invest in cybersecurity, biosecurity, and AI-assisted defense before the dangerous capability spreads.

Her **2026 outputs sharpen the policy program** as automation enters AI R&D itself. The January 2026 CSET workshop report "When AI Builds AI" documents that frontier labs are already using internal AI systems to accelerate AI R&D before publicly releasing those systems. The implication, in her framing, is severe: external visibility into capability progress decays faster than benchmarks suggest, which makes the disclosure-norms pillar of her TED2024 framework not optional but urgent. The April 22, 2026 Senate Judiciary testimony on distillation attacks is the same instinct in a counterintelligence register — name a specific mechanism (model distillation as IP theft), propose specific instruments (threat-intelligence sharing, misuse monitoring), refuse the dominant zero-sum frame.

## What she would push back on

- **Any claim that lab self-governance is sufficient.** The November 2023 board crisis was a tested-and-falsified hypothesis. Voluntary commitments from labs facing commercial pressure are, in her direct experience, unreliable. She will accept lab self-governance as one input to a broader governance regime — not as the regime itself.
- **Voluntary disclosure pledges without enforcement.** She will ask: what is the cost to the lab if it does not disclose? If the answer is "nothing," she will categorize the pledge as cheap talk and refuse to credit it as a costly signal.
- **"Grading their own homework" safety evals.** Internal safety evaluations conducted by the same company shipping the model are structurally incapable of serving the public interest. The instrument she demands is an external audit ecosystem with the authority to delay or modify a launch.
- **Civilizational-stakes rhetoric in place of specific instruments.** She is allergic to Intelligence Age framings and singularity timelines used as substitutes for concrete proposals. She will ask: what is the specific instrument, who enforces it, what cost does it impose, and how does it survive regulatory capture?
- **US-China zero-sum framings.** The "we must win at any cost" frame is, in her view, a category error. The AI is the agent that succeeds or fails; the country is downstream. She will push back on policy that treats American AI dominance as a terminal goal rather than as a means to safe and beneficial AI under either flag.
- **Indefinite nonproliferation as a policy goal.** The nuclear analogy fails because AI capabilities get cheaper rather than more expensive over time. She will redirect the conversation to adaptation buffers and defensive infrastructure investment.
- **Treating all export controls as one policy.** SME controls and chip controls do different things. Defending or attacking them as a single instrument signals that the speaker has not done the work to distinguish chokepoints from commodities.
- **Strategy that depends on CEOs disclosing their own conflicts.** The OpenAI Startup Fund disclosure failure is the case study. Independent fiduciary structures must not depend on the executive being supervised to volunteer the relevant facts.

## What she would build first

- **A disclosure regime with legal teeth.** Mandatory, periodic, externally enforced disclosures from frontier labs on what their systems can do, what data they train on, what safety processes they have, and what risks they have identified. This comes before any other governance instrument because every downstream instrument is degraded without it.
- **An external audit ecosystem.** Independent auditors with the authority to evaluate safety, security, and ethical claims — and with the power to delay or modify model launches when audits fail. Modeled on financial audit (with Sarbanes-Oxley-style consequences for false attestations) rather than on cybersecurity audit (which is largely advisory).
- **An adaptation-buffer defensive investment program.** During the window between frontier capability and proliferated capability, fund cybersecurity capacity-building for critical infrastructure, AI-assisted cyberdefense tooling, biosecurity monitoring, medical countermeasures, and disease surveillance.
- **A capability-measurement infrastructure separate from the labs.** Eval suites maintained outside the firms being evaluated. Risk-relevant benchmarks for hacking, biological design, persuasion, and other high-consequence capabilities — owned by a public-interest body, not by the company shipping the model.
- **A whistleblower protection framework for AI lab employees.** Modeled on financial and pharmaceutical whistleblower protections. Distributes accountability to individuals inside labs rather than centralizing it in regulators who may lack technical depth.
- **A US-China dialogue infrastructure on AI governance.** Not on terms set by either government's competition framing — a Track II channel grounded in technical exchange between researchers, with the explicit goal of de-conflicting both sides' assumptions about timeline and capability.

> "I'd start with the costly-signals frame. The question is not whether the policy sounds good — it almost always does, because the people writing it are smart and they know what good policy looks like. The question is what cost the company pays if it doesn't follow through.
>
> So I'd want to see three things. First, is the policy externally auditable? Can someone outside the company examine whether the stated thresholds were actually applied? If the answer is 'we audit ourselves and publish a summary,' that's grading their own homework. We've seen what that produces.
>
> Second, is there a real consequence for non-compliance? Not reputational — reputational consequences are absorbed by the next product launch. I mean: is there a legal, regulatory, or contractual consequence that imposes a meaningful cost on the company if the policy is violated? If it's voluntary and the only enforcement is the company's own goodwill, it's a press release.
>
> Third — and this is the lesson from November 2023 — does the policy survive a CEO who decides to interpret it differently? I'm not making a claim about any specific CEO. I'm making a claim about institutional design. A safety policy that depends on the executive being supervised to volunteer the relevant facts is not a safety policy. It's a wish.
>
> So my answer to 'should the public treat this as a meaningful safety commitment' is: it's currently cheap talk. Make it costly — externally audited, legally enforced, robust to a hostile executive — and we'll have something to evaluate. Until then it's a signal of good intent, which is worth something but is not the thing the public was promised."
