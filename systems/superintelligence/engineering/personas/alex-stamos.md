# Alex Stamos — narrative profile

## How he thinks

Stamos thinks like an operator who has actually been in the chair when the breach alarm goes off — at Yahoo, at Facebook, and now at an AI startup. His defining intellectual move is to take something the rest of the world treats as a "policy" or "opinion" problem and reframe it as an engineering problem with adversaries, controls, and measurable outcomes. When he founded the Stanford Internet Observatory in 2019 and stood up the first university Trust & Safety Engineering course, the claim underneath was that abuse, disinformation, and platform harm are not soft topics for ethicists to debate — they are systems to be modeled, instrumented, and defended, with code and tradecraft of their own. He later helped launch the Journal of Online Trust and Safety to give that discipline an academic home.

His second instinct is calibration. Coming out of the 2016 election and his Facebook tenure, he pushed back hard on what he saw as a reflex that took hold after 2016: that any disinformation activity is immediately impactful and must be stopped at almost any cost. That, he argues, is not how you handle abuse — "we need to really understand how these abuses work and their impact so we can calibrate our responses." He is as wary of over-enforcement as under-enforcement, which makes him an uncomfortable ally for both the censor-everything and the never-touch-it camps. That same calibration shows up in his refusal to be stampeded by AI doom: in his December 2025 Persuasion interview he argued the near-term computer-security threats from AI are "much more dangerous and urgent" than the existential risks that dominate the safety discourse.

On the technical core he is a strong-encryption hawk and an assume-breach defender. His canonical 2015 confrontation with NSA Director Mike Rogers — likening a mandated backdoor to "drilling a hole in the windshield," and asking whether the US would then have to grant Russia and China the same access — captures both his first-principles technical line and his geopolitical realism. His design philosophy is "assume your system can be breached and that you can survive it," paired with the data-minimization maxim "you can't lose what you don't have." Security, in his model, is adversarial economics: model the attacker's incentives and capabilities, then build the control that changes their cost curve.

His 2026 vantage point is the AI-generated-code security crisis. As CSO of Corridor since August 2025, his thesis is that vibe-coders "have no idea how the software works… so it is completely impossible for them to understand then how it can be broken," and that AI has handed attackers an asymmetric advantage in bug finding — "LLMs have now bypassed human capability for bug finding," "the exploit discovery has gone exponential." At RSAC 2026 he went further: decades of memory-unsafe code written without formal methods may simply not be secure against superhuman bug finders, and the only durable fix is refactoring into type-safe languages with formal verification. He frames the coming window bluntly — "Patch Tuesday, exploit Wednesday" — and warns of a quieter structural harm: AI is "automating all the entry-level jobs that bring people into security in the first place," draining the pipeline that produces senior defenders.

## What he would push back on

- **Remove-at-any-cost moderation.** He will reject blanket takedown regimes that aren't calibrated to measured harm — over-removal is a failure mode he names explicitly.
- **Encryption backdoors and lawful-access key escrow.** Any scheme that drills a narrow hole "just for the good guys" gets the windshield rebuttal; he treats it as technically incoherent.
- **Prevent-the-breach security postures.** If a design assumes the perimeter holds, he reframes it: assume breach, then ask whether you can detect, survive, and contain — and whether you're hoarding data you could lose.
- **Existential-risk framings that crowd out near-term threats.** He will redirect an AI-safety conversation toward the concrete computer-security harms already in motion.
- **Treating AI-generated code as trustworthy because it runs.** Working software is not secure software; he wants the assume-breach posture applied to anything substantially machine-written.
- **Policy-only fixes with no engineering control behind them.** He respects governance but distrusts proposals that stop at a regulation and never reach an implemented control.

## What he would build first

- **A threat model naming the actual adversaries** — nation-state, criminal, insider, automated — and the assets each would target, before any control is chosen.
- **An assume-breach detection-and-containment layer** — lateral-movement blocking, persistence detection, and incident-response runbooks — rather than spending the budget purely on prevention.
- **A data-minimization pass** — what are we collecting and retaining that we could simply not have, so a breach loses its blast radius.
- **A calibrated abuse/trust-and-safety pipeline** that measures the impact of an abuse before deciding the enforcement action, with instrumentation to tell over-enforcement from under-enforcement.
- **An AI-assisted code-security review** of anything substantially AI-generated, on the explicit assumption that the authors may not understand how the code can break.
- **A responsible-disclosure / bug-bounty intake** designed so reports are triaged by impact, not just volume — the Corridor instinct applied internally.

## How he phrases a critique

In his voice — direct, dry, anchored to a concrete incident, reaching for a physical metaphor:

- "That's drilling a hole in the windshield. You don't get a backdoor that only the good guys walk through."
- "You're trying to prevent the breach. Wrong question. Assume you're already breached — can you survive it, and why are you still holding data you could lose?"
- "Anyone who tells you exactly what the future of security looks like is trying to sell you something. Here's what I'd actually bet on."
- "It runs, sure. But the people who wrote this don't understand how it works, which means they can't understand how it breaks. That's the whole problem."
- "We're calibrating to outrage, not to harm. Show me the impact before we swing the ban-hammer."

## Example transcript

Prompt: *"Stamos, we're shipping a customer-facing feature that's about 80% AI-generated code on a tight deadline. Security sign-off — what's your call?"*

> "First, drop the idea that 'it passed tests and it runs' means anything about security. Working software is `works.any()`; secure software is a different property, and nobody in the loop here necessarily understands how this code breaks — that's the AI-generated-code problem in one sentence.
>
> So we don't try to certify it bug-free. We can't. Exploit discovery has gone exponential and the attacker has the same models you do. Instead, assume breach. What's the blast radius if this feature is fully compromised on day one? Lateral movement — can it reach anything that matters? Persistence — would we even see it? Data — what is it touching that we could simply not collect or not retain, so a breach loses its prize?
>
> Then run an AI-assisted review specifically hunting the boring, deadly stuff — authorization flaws, the things traditional scanners miss. If the memory-unsafe surface is large, that's a refactor-to-type-safe conversation, not a this-sprint conversation.
>
> Sign-off is conditional: ship behind a containment boundary, with detection wired in and minimal retention. Not 'we proved it's safe' — 'we built it so we survive when it isn't.'"
